Do social sciences and humanities behave like life and hard sciences ? Andrea Bonaccorsi 1 , 2 • Cinzia Daraio 3 • Stefano Fantoni 4 • Viola Folli 5 • Marco Leonetti 5 , 7 • Giancarlo Ruocco 5 , 6 Received : 12 January 2017 (cid:2) Akade´miai Kiado´ , Budapest , Hungary 2017 Abstract The quantitative evaluation of Social Science and Humanities ( SSH ) and the investigation of the existing similarities between SSH and Life and Hard Sciences ( LHS ) represent the forefront of scientometrics research . We analyse the scientiﬁc production of the universe of Italian academic scholars , over a 10 - year period across 2002 – 2012 , from a national database built by the Italian National Agency for the Evaluation of Universities and Research Institutes . We demonstrate that all Italian scholars of SSH and LHS are equals , as far as their publishing habits . They share the same general law , which is a lognormal . At the same time , however , they are different , because we measured their scientiﬁc production with different indicators required by the Italian law ; we eliminated the ‘‘silent’’ scholars and obtained different scaling values—proxy of their productivity rates . Our ﬁndings may be useful to further develop indirect quali – quantitative comparative analysis across heterogeneous disciplines and , more broadly , to investigate on the generative mechanisms behind the observed empirical regularities . Up till a few years ago , the Social Sciences and Humanities ( SSH ) had not often been subjected to such analyses . At the same time , there has been an increased request from academic authorities of many universities and governmental / in - dependent agencies in charge of evaluation to get a quantitative overview of the perfor - mance of research in all ﬁelds of science . Indeed , it is important that those who are involved in the development of performance indicators for the assessment of Social Sciences and Humanities should take into account the characteristics of these domains , their substantive content and in particular the practices of communication between scholars and the structure of the communication system ( see Moed 2005 ) . It is widely recognised that SSH disciplines , with some exceptions , share the following differences compared to LHS : lower incidence of scientiﬁc journals in the total production and signiﬁcant weight given to monographs ; wider use of the national language ; reduced frequency of publication ; different cited sources and age distribution of citations ( see the classical work of Hicks 2004 ; and more recently Huang and Chang 2008 ) . However , it has been recently recognised that some parts of Humanities ‘‘function quite similar to the LHS : for instance , the discipline of Linguistics where it is quite common to publish regularly in and cite articles in fairly high - impact journals’’ ( see Zuccala ( 2013 ) , and the references cited there ) . Nevertheless , main differences in publication and citation behaviour between the LHS and the SSH have been found in the literature ( Nederhof 2006 ) and the limits of existing databases for assessing SSH have been highlighted ( Archambault et al . 2006 ) . However , there have been growing attempts to examine the validity of the application of classical bibliometric analyses for assessing SSH ( see e . g . van Leeuwen 2006 ) ; expanding citation analysis and the use of lifetime citation data , as well as the addition of library holdings ( Linmans 2010 ) ; exploring citation analysis on minority languages studies in humanities ( Ardanuy et al . 2009 ) . Moreover , an increasing number of works are proposing the application of bibliometric techniques to library catalogs ( Torres - Salinas and Moed 2009 ) and book publications ( White et al . 2009 ) . Recently , the evaluation of SSH has witnessed new advancements including the assessment of books and their integration with other metrics ( e . g . Gime´nez - Toledo et al . 2016 ; Zuccala and Cornacchia 2016 ) , including alternative metrics , such as Mendeley ( e . g . Mohammadi and Thelwall 2014 ) . Several papers ( Uzzi and Spiro 2005 ; Uzzi et al . 2013 ; Wuchty et al . 2007 ; Guimera et al . 2005 ) , showed similar results not only for LHS , but also for SSH . Verleysen and Weeren ( 2016 ) analysing 1828 scholars from ﬁve Belgian universities in 16 disciplines by cluster analysis , found that ‘‘diversity in SSH publication patterns cuts across disciplinary boundaries’’ . These results show that all sciences seem to share some common universal regularities . However , these results are based on data that have a poor coverage of the whole domain of SSH ( see also e . g . Archambault et al . 2006 ; Norris and Oppenheim 2007 ) . Bibliometric databases , such as Web of Science ( WoS ) and Scopus are increasing their coverage ; see e . g . the Book indexing initiative of Scopus , although their coverage is far for being complete . Other recent works , however , have found that different scientiﬁc ﬁelds may cultivate different attitude in their research strategies ( see e . g . Jaffe 2014 ) and show differences in some quantiﬁable structural characteristics of the papers , such as number of authors , length of references and so on ( see e . g . Fanelli and Gla¨nzel 2013 ) . Scientometrics 123 The distributions of bibliometric indicators , such as publications and citations , have been investigated since the inﬂuential work of Lotka ( 1926 ) , up to recent years ( see e . g . Seglen 1992 ; van Raan 2006 ) , ﬁnding highly skewed distributions , often power laws , discussed at length in the literature ( see e . g . Egghe and Rousseau 1990 , 1996 ) . An highly skewed scientiﬁc production determines a non - linear increase ( cumulative advantage ) of the impact measured by means of citations as a function of publications , showing a power - law dependence with an exponent larger than one ( see e . g . van Raan 2008 ) . This means that , as found e . g . by van Raan ( 2008 ) , at the group level of analysis , citations ‘‘scales’’ in a disproportional fashion , in a non - linear way according to a power law . This ‘‘scaling’’ behaviour is of course different from the scaling approach we apply in the present paper . Generally speaking , the scaling approach we pursue here is based on looking for ‘‘uni - versal’’ or master curves of bibliometric parameters which may allow us to compare different heterogeneous disciplines at the level of the scholar’s scientiﬁc production . A scaling approach to investigate the distributions of bibliometric indicators has been proposed by Radicchi et al . ( 2008 ) , looking at the distribution of citations of papers . Their level of analysis concerned the single paper ( not the scholars’ scientiﬁc production ) . They found a universal log - normal density distribution across sub - ﬁelds included in the WoS database ( see also Evans et al . 2012 ) . Other studies , at the same level of analysis , for instance Stringer et al . ( 2008 , 2010 ) , found that ‘‘virtually all ﬁelds ( including SSH as indexed in the WoS database ) had a log - normal distribution of citations after the papers had enough time to accrue citations’’ . Whilst there is a growing evidence on the universality of bibliometric parameters in the LHS ( e . g . Radicchi et al . 2008 ; Evans et al . 2012 ) , the proof of the existence of scaling parameters to compare heterogeneous academic disciplines in the LHS is very recent ( see Ruocco and Daraio 2013 ) . Log - normal distributions have indeed been found in several ﬁelds of applications ( see e . g . Limpert et al . 2001 ; Martinez - Mekler et al . 2009 ) . Waltman et al . ( 2012 ) , however , propose a large scale validation of the universality of citation distributions , including also social sciences and they ﬁnd that not all ﬁelds share the same master curve . In particular , ﬁelds like engineering sciences , material sciences and social sciences seem to have non - universal citation distributions . However , they note : ‘‘univer - sality claim becomes more justiﬁable when uncited publications are excluded from the analysis’’ ( Waltman et al . 2012 , p . 76 ) . The contribution that we bring to the current literature is the demonstration that , besides the different research strategies of the different disciplines ( see e . g . Jaffe 2014 ) , there is a similarity in the distribution of the publishing behaviours of individual scholars across all the sciences , from theoretical physics to Latin language and literature , from molecular biology to law . Indeed , the sciences all share the same general law that is a log - normal . At the same time , they differ because we measured their scientiﬁc production with different indicators required by the Italian law ; we eliminated the silent scholars and obtained different scaling values , which are proxy of their productivity rates . Ruocco and Daraio ( 2013 ) showed that a scaling approach , typical of statistical mechanics in physics , can be useful to compare the scientiﬁc production of heterogeneous academic disciplines . Differently from previous works that applied a scaling approach to investigate the distributions of bibliometric indicators ( Radicchi et al . 2008 ; Evans et al . 2012 ) , they applied a scaling approach on percentile rankings built on bibliometric indi - cators , and investigated the existence of scaling by considering the cumulative distributions of the bibliometric parameters , instead of their densities . From an empirical perspective , they show that if the ( heterogeneous ) bibliometric distributions of a scientiﬁc parameter ( e . g . number of publications , citations and / or Impact factors ) of different academic ﬁelds Scientometrics 123 collapse into a unique master curve , which differs only for a single scaling parameter , then , the scaling factors estimated for each academic ﬁeld , represent a comparable value , a kind of ‘‘exchange rate’’ , of the parameter studied of one discipline with respect to other disciplines . This paper extends the approach proposed in Ruocco and Daraio ( 2013 ) to a wider sample of LHS disciplines and to the SSH disciplines . The Italian academic system is an interesting empirical case for an international audience for at least three reasons . Italy is a big science producer in the world’s scientiﬁc landscape . The Scimago Country ranking compares the scientiﬁc production as indexed in the Scopus database across 228 countries in the world . According to the 2015 Scimago ranking , 1 Italy is the 8th country in the world for total documents produced ( 95 , 836 , after USA , China , UK , Germany , India , Japan and France ) , 6th for total number of citations ( 61 , 007 , after USA , China , UK , Germany and France ) and 7th for its h - index ( 766 , after USA , UK , Germany , France , Canada , Japan ) . Secondly , research assessment in Italy has been the object of deep changes after the introduction of the ‘‘controversial’’ university reform ( see e . g . Cartlidge 2010 ) and the setting up of the National Agency for the Evaluation of the University and Research System ( ANVUR ) . These had the objective of linking the assessment to the performance and develop a system of assessment of the quality of research ( see e . g . Owens 2013 ) . Thirdly , an interesting feature of the Italian university system is the distinction , made by the Italian law , between ‘‘bibliometric ( hard ) ’’ and ‘‘non bibliometric ( soft ) ’’ disciplines , according to the Ministerial Decree n . 76 of 2012 ( Regulation laying down criteria and parameters for the evaluation of candidates for the national scientiﬁc qualiﬁcation ) . Annex A of the Decree distinguishes between : • Bibliometric disciplines , whose scientiﬁc production consists mainly of a number of papers indexed in Scopus or WoS and citations ; • Non bibliometric disciplines , whose scientiﬁc production consists primarily of a number of articles or chapters in books with ISBN and number of articles in the so called Class A . The Italian legislator , recognising that the publication pattern of Psychology is much more in line with Medicine than Social Sciences and that that of Architecture is much closer to Social Sciences than Engineering , considers Psychology as hard or bibliometric discipline while Design and Architecture disciplines are classiﬁed as soft or non bibliometric . Moreover , in Italy each scholar belongs to a disciplinary sector ( called hereafter SSD which stands for Settore Scientiﬁco Disciplinare , see ‘‘Appendix 1’’ for a list of the analysed SSDs ) . This ofﬁcial classiﬁcation of the whole academic staff by discipline offers a unique opportunity to investigate and compare the publication behaviour at a detailed disaggregated level , without requiring a subjective proﬁling analysis for each scholar . The only distinction we made across disciplines in the paper is based on the different publishing indicators collected at institutional level , according to the existing law , and hence available for the current paper . Summing up , in the present paper , we : • Frame the applied scaling approach and the obtained results in the context of recent developments in the comparison of LHS versus SSH ( Stringer et al . 2008 , 2010 ; Gime´nez - Toledo et al . 2016 ; Verleysen and Weeren 2016 ; Uzzi and Spiro 2005 ; Uzzi et al . 2013 ; Wuchty et al . 2007 ; Guimera et al . 2005 ) ; • Pave the way for a new indirect quali – quantitative comparative analysis across heterogeneous ﬁelds and based on heterogeneous sources , combining traditional , institutional and future possible altmetrics - based indicators . The paper is organized as follows . The next section presents the data analysed . After that , we introduce the method of the analysis . The following section introduces the main results and the subsequent section presents the discussion and concludes the paper . Data We exploit the construction of a dataset covering all academic staff in all Italian univer - sities for the 10 year period across 2002 – 2012 . The dataset was built by the Italian National Agency for the Evaluation of the University and Research System ( ANVUR ) within a legislative framework aimed at changing the procedures for the promotion to Associate professor and Full professor ( Science 330 ( 2010 ) , 1462 ) . The Agency came into operation in 2011 ( after a long preparation process described in Nature 468 ( 2010 ) , 1002 and Nature 502 ( 2013 ) , 288 – 290 ) and started a large research assessment exercise , called VQR ( Valutazione della Qualita´ della Ricerca ) , published in 2013 . In parallel , the Agency was requested by the Ministry of University and Research to create a system of indicators which could be used as minimal requirement for academic promotion . This request came amidst large debate about the transparency of academic promotions , in a system which had been described as characterized by ‘‘a marked resis - tance to the evaluation of scientiﬁc output’’ ( Nature , 456 ( 2008 ) , 166 ) . A detailed description of the dataset , of the procedure for its construction and of data quality is offered in ‘‘Appendix 1’’ . With respect to SSH the dataset gives , for the 10 - year period across 2002 – 2012 , for all academic staff in service ( Associate and Full professors ) , Scientometrics 123 three indicators : the number of books , the sum of book chapters and journal articles , and the number of articles in A - rated journals . The ﬁrst and second are classical count indicators , which may beinterpreted as volumeor quantityindicators . In particular , noattemptwas made to evaluate the quality of books or book chapters . The third one might be considered as a very rough indicator of quality of research ( at least in comparison with the previous indicator ) , since A - rated journals are the result of a massive classiﬁcation effort based on expert opinion andrepresent 10 – 20 % ofthe total number of journalsin which Italian scholars have published in the period ( Ferrara and Bonaccorsi 2016 ; ‘‘Appendix 2’’ for all details ) . Therefore the three indicators taken together offer , for the ﬁrst time , a rough picture of both quantity and quality of scientiﬁc production of SSH . Among them , we concentrate here on the second and third indicators , not studying the number of books , which is an additional wildly ﬂuctuating quantity indicator , due to the small numbers of observations . For the sake of comparison , we also investigate , for the SSDs belonging to LHS , two out of the three indicators introduced by ANVUR : the number of papers published in indexed journals and the citations collected . In the last column , LHS and SSH indicate ‘‘Life and Hard Science’’ and ‘‘Social Science and Humanities’’ respectively . The data on Full and Associate Professors were collected at the institutional level for estimating threshold , namely medians , to access the national qualiﬁcation ( see also the ‘‘Appendix 2’’ with additional data information ) . We know that productivity and impact are related to career stage ( e . g . Deville et al . 2014 ) and for that reason the rough measures of Full and Associate Professors considered here fail to capture tenure in an accurate way . However , we think that these rough measures can be useful for the purpose of our analysis , which is looking for a general master curve to allow for a comparative analysis across heterogeneous ﬁelds on the basis of different data sources that , even though approximate , might better capture the aggregate behaviour of the analysed scientiﬁc production and allow for setting thresholds to access the national qualiﬁcation . Method Ruocco and Daraio ( 2013 ) proposed a ‘‘scaling’’ approach to compare the scientiﬁc per - formance of Italian heterogeneous academic disciplines belonging to LHS . The bibliometric parameters considered there were : ( i ) the total number of papers published in a given time interval , ( ii ) the total number of citations to the papers in ( i ) , and , ( iii ) , the sum of the Impact Factor ( IF ) of ( the journals of ) the papers in ( i ) . The data were collected from the Web of Science database . Although it is well known that IF is inappropriate for measuring the performance of individual publications of a researcher ( see e . g . Moed and Leeuwen 1996 ; more recently , the Leiden Manifesto by Hicks et al . 2015 ) , Ruocco and Daraio ( 2013 ) showed that the IF and the number of citations had a very high correlation in the Italian case they investigated . Such a common distribution turns out to be the Log - Normal distribution . The existence and uniqueness of scaling implies that we can compare different academic disciplines in terms of their scientiﬁc performance on a common ground . In spite of the marked dif - ferences existing between various disciplines , such uniqueness , if proved as we claim , shows that there is a common ground of their publishing behaviors . Deviations from this common ground , and consequently from the universal scaling curves , are particularly important because they signal anomalies in the production , such as clustering phenomena or others , that need further insight . See the last section of this paper for a discussion on the possible reasons for such common behaviors . Therefore , the idea behind this approach , which we extend here to cover also SSH , does not necessarily imply that different scientiﬁc ﬁelds can be reduced to a single rational model of scientiﬁc production . There are , indeed , several possible sources of heterogeneity among academic ﬁelds’ production , namely : • LHS and SSH differ for the kind of publication of their research outputs . Here we extend the approach of Ruocco and Daraio ( 2013 ) to SSH , by considering as proxy of quantity x 3 ( number of articles or chapters in books with ISBN ) and proxy of quality indicator x 4 ( articles in A - rated journals ) . These indicators are compared against x 1 ( number of articles ) and x 2 ( number of citations ) for the LHS . • The productivity rate of the scientiﬁc production differs across SSDs due to differences in the habits , timings , communication practices of research results and so on . • The number of silent scholars is different across SSDs . Because of different pressures , competitiveness and complexity faced , dissimilar disci - plines show different behaviour and practices in the publication of their research outputs . However , what we show here is that there is a universal law capable of describing the general pattern of the distribution of the bibliometric indicators of the different academic ﬁelds from both LHS and SSH . This ﬁnding provides a common framework for comparison across sciences . Discussion and conclusions This paper gives a contribution to the ‘‘science of science’’ , establishing a close similarity among the publishing habits of the researchers of diverse disciplines . This allows one to extend and adapt the typical paradigms and methodology of the LHS also to SSH and vice versa . This is the ﬁrst time , to the best of our knowledge , that the total production of scholars in SSH , in a large country ( Italy ) covering a long period ( one decade ) and with a ﬁne - grained disciplinary background , has been elaborated for purposes of indirect comparative analysis across disciplines . The paper shows that remarkable differences in the microstructure of scientiﬁc com - munication do not translate into different aggregate statistical properties . The compelling result of our paper is that Italian scholars of very heterogeneous dis - ciplines are somewhat different , which forced us to measure their publications with dif - ferent indicators . However , the different indicators used , the elimination of silent scholars and the different scaling values ( proxy of their productivity rates ) obtained allowed us to compare equivalent aspects among them , allowing for an equal treatment of their academic achievements . In perspective , the comparative approach developed in this paper could be applied also beyond the Italian context in which it developed , thanks to the increasing availability of institutional comprehensive data ( not limited to the traditional WoS or Scopus only database ) for other countries ( e . g . Norway , see Rørstad and Aksnes 2015 ) . Skewed distributions have long been observed in LHS . The standard explanation for skewness is some variant of Robert K . Merton’s Matthew effect ( Merton 1968 ) . The explanation is as follows : cumulative effects are at play in the form of scientiﬁc recog - nition . Scientists who are able to achieve discoveries earlier in their career get recognition and receive more funding , hire more assistants and students , build up laboratories , pursue more directions of research leading to more discoveries . Over time , these initial differences are not wiped out but cumulatively self - reinforce . It is important to remark that the overall Scientometrics 123 theory of cumulative advantages has been built upon hard science . Two elements are crucial for the mechanism to operate : recognition goes to scientists on the basis of priority of discovery , and research cannot be pursued without large funding . While these elements describe research in LHS adequately , what about SSH ? A theory of cumulative advantages in SSH needs to be created . Why do scholars in SSH behave like those in LHS ? Are they subject to the same kind of competition and depend on research funding exactly as LHS ? What is the main common ground , the propagative mechanism that generates the skewed distributions empirically observed in both SSH and LHS ? This is a puzzling question that deserves to be addressed by further research . If we assume that it is recognition , more than competition , which triggers a cumulative increase in the scientiﬁc productivity of scholars across all the disciplines , then we can link our results to the framework proposed by Whitley ( 2000 ) to develop comparative analysis among SSH and LHS . According to Whitley ( 2000 ) different scientiﬁc ﬁelds which follow diverse formal systems of production of knowledge can be comparatively analysed on the base of a common meta factor which is reputation : heterogeneous scientiﬁc ﬁelds can be compared on a common ground as they are ‘‘reputational work organizations’’ . These reputational organizations are structurally different across academic systems and broader institutional contexts , and are subject to different contextual environments . In this paper we found , at aggregate level , that the indicators of scientiﬁc performance of most academic ﬁelds of SSH and LHS follow a Log - Normal distribution , although they differ for their kind of products ( articles vs articles and chapters ; citations vs articles in A - rated journals ) , productivity rate and number of silent scholars . It is well known that Log - Normal distributions are associated to multiplicative random phenomena , in which heterogeneous conditions and factors generate skewed distributions . According to our interpretation , it seems that the prevailing common factor , reputation , leads to a common framework for the comparative analysis of the performance across heterogeneous academic ﬁelds of LHS and SSH . However , we are still lacking a detailed and convincing ab - initio model able to explain how reputation could give rise to the observed Log - Normal distributions . The results of this paper show that there is a convergence between the organizational sociology and the quantitative investigation of the sciences , and this could open the way to a new stream of ‘‘ indirect’’ quali - quantitative investigations which could greatly increase our understanding of the different intellectual and organizational functioning of hetero - geneous disciplines within a uniﬁed framework . Institutions 93 61 Scientometrics 123 Appendix 2 : Sources and nature of data Law no . 240 / 2010 has changed radically the way Italian professors have to be promoted , by creating a dual system of National Scientiﬁc Habilitation and local recruitment . The Ministerial Decree no . 76 / 2012 established that quantititive indicators should be used to qualify minimum requirements for candidates and delegated ANVUR to produce such indicators . On June 2012 ANVUR published in its website the Deliberation no . 50 / 2012 , which speciﬁed in great detail the procedures for the computation of indicators based on publications . Based on the legislation that mandated the general criteria for the procedure , ANVUR announced that indicators would have been constructed on the basis of the data included by scholars in their personal homepage , called ‘‘loginmiur’’ . With respect to the quality of data available , the following remarks are relevant . Individual scholars are responsible for updating the information . No administrative consequence or sanction is considered for not updating or ﬁlling in wrong information . The control on the quality of data provided by Cineca is a formal one . This means that Cineca , using automatic computer queries , ensures that the data uploaded by scholars are consistent with formal deﬁnitions . Among the formal controls there are the following : • for books , the ISBN code is requested and the distinction between author and editor is recorded ; • for journals , the ISSN code is requested ; • all articles from journals are assigned to journals whose metadata are found in a Master list , managed by Cineca , based on controls of the titles ; • if a new journal title is included in the homepage , the system does not record the metadata until the new journal is recognized and included in the Master list . With respect to the quality of data , we must distinguish between books and journal articles . With respect to books , the actual content of the category may include non - academic books . If a book has a ISBN code , its metadata are recorded in the homepage without ﬁlters . This means that the nature of the book recorded is left to the determination of scholars . While the homepage is clearly aimed at academic purposes , many scholars tend to use it as a personal repository for all their publications , including popular science , professional texts , books aimed at a political or general audience , and the like . While Deliberation 50 asked scholars to update only books of academic type , it is impossible to give an estimate of the inﬂation represented by non - academic books . On the contrary , for journal articles there has been a massive work of classiﬁcation made by ANVUR upon mandate of the Decree 76 / 2012 . In particular , among journals included in personal homepages , ANVUR selected those considered , following the same Deliberation n . 50 , ‘‘scientiﬁc’’ and excluded non - scientiﬁc ones . Among scientiﬁc journals a small set ( approximately 12 % of the total ) was included in the category of A - rated journals . As a consequence , for journals the possibility of inﬂation of data is almost non - existent , since all data have been ﬁltered using formally approved lists of journals . With respect to the issues of quality of data , it is useful to report two quality control procedures . These may give a hint of the magnitude of the problem of data quality , while they do not resolve the problem once and for all . The ﬁrst is a quality control of the data on books carried out by ANVUR during the procedure . In fact , it was possible for Full professors who were denied the access to the Habilitation panel in the ﬁrst round to submit an appeal . After receiving the appeal , ANVUR re - examined all publications in order to verify whether there were errors or , most frequently omissions ( in particular , omissions of actually existing ISBN codes ) in the application , so that the candidate actually met the thresholds and could eventually be admitted , or rather there was a conﬁrmation of the rejection . During the appeal procedures , the publications of more than 1500 Full professors were examined manually , of which 800 from SSH . Contrary to initial expectations , the cases in which the books declared in the homepages were clearly non - academic were extremely rare ( among them , one case of Scientometrics 123 tourist guide was prominent , however ) . In almost all cases , controversies were related to the following issues : date of publication ( e . g . Professor (cid:7) declares the book Y as published in year 2011 , so that it is computed in the indicator , but then it is found that the date of publication has been postponed , or even it is not yet published ) —wrong ISBN ( e . g . Pro - fessor J includes a number not corresponding with the title of the book , leaving open the question whether it is a mistake or a fraud , i . e . the book does not have a ISBN code ) — dubious academic qualiﬁcation of publishers ( e . g . the publisher is not member of a list of publishers associated to the National Association ( AIE ) , has not a website or ofﬁcial address ) . It must be remembered that in the Italian legislation a provision for the precise qualiﬁcation of ‘‘scientiﬁc book’’ is found , following a long - awaited initiative to create a transparent and open public archive of scientiﬁc publications established in 2009 . How - ever , this legislative provision has never been materialized , so that the concept of ‘‘sci - entiﬁc book’’ is left empty of speciﬁc content . Nevertheless , almost all cases treated during the appeal procedure had to deal with controversies over books whose academic content was beyond dispute . This does not exclude the fact that non - academic books have inﬂated the number of books in general . In fact , appeal procedures were typically submitted by Full professors whose overall production was below the thresholds by a little margin , so that recognizing even a small number of previously excluded publications might reverse the outcome . It is still possible that the list of books of those admitted since the beginning include either scientiﬁc and non - scientiﬁc books . The second quality procedure was put in place immediately after the publication of the Deliberation n . 50 , in order to check whether some senior scholar would cancel their full list in protest , perhaps with a goal of diminishing the aggregate ﬁgures . In order to detect this possibility , ANVUR controlled the ﬁgures of individual scholars daily , using a routine algorithm developed by Cineca .
