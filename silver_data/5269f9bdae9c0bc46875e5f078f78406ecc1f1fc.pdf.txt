Developing a system for social control and use is a complex problem . It spans three already complex entities , i . e . , users , devices , and spaces . It should consider the heterogeneity of the entities and their potential intricate interactions . A public space further compounds the situation . It inherently bears uncertainties : visitors freely enter and exit different spaces at any time at their own will . It is unpredictable who the visitors are and when they visit . They are strangers to each other . Similarly , they confront actuator devices in an unexpected way , i . e . , it is not certain if and what they will meet in the space . It is unclear who is eligible to engage in the control . Synchronous interactions among the visitors are almost infeasible . This complexity and uncertainty would preclude the adoption of simple and common strategies for system design . The proposed Social Control - and - Use Architecture aims at pro - viding a ground upon which efforts for challenging system design could be performed and elaborated . It is developed as a 5 - layered architecture after rounds of thoughtful and exploratory applications of the principle of separation of concerns ; the separation of delicate human social interactions from intricate device connectivity ; the separation of awareness of social contexts and formulation of pref - erences from legitimate consensus - building process ; the separation of authorized participants from potential ones , etc . With unraveling of the source of complexity , five core components are identified composing the layered architecture ( Â§ 3 ) . This initial architecture would guide respective designers to look into the complication and intricacy in their own problems and reach an effective system design . Hivemind is a novel system which transforms an exclusively - controlled actuator in a public space into a true public actuator . Designed as a specific articulation of the 5 - layered architecture towards the high uncertainties of the public space , it allows vis - itors to instantly participate in the democratic collective control of the actuator . While efficiency and fairness are effective in most cases , they sometimes do not operate in the desired manner . We consider the following two limitations . Oscillation problem : During a micro - deployment , we observed an oscillation of actuator states . An IoT speaker , which followed the fairness policy , kept swinging between two playlists every few seconds . It turned out that these playlists were selected by the two least satisfied visitors . This oscillation made the songs stop during their introduction . This significantly degraded user satisfaction . Space - specific restriction : Public spaces often have space - specific restrictions . The restrictions may include turning off the lights at night , limiting the AC temperature to save energy , and not playing the music too loud . They are managed by space admins or have already been a consensus between people . For example , consider lobby lights in a guesthouse . Guesthouse owners may want their guests to turn off the lobby lights during bedtime . This intention can be translated into a new restriction turning off the lobby lights un - less the guests are unanimous . These restrictions , however , cannot be applied to Hivemind only with the two primitives . To address the oscillation problem and support space - specific restrictions , Hivemind introduces state precedence . State precedence has two main functions . First , it grants a temporary high priority on the current state , thereby preventing the oscillation problem . For example , Hivemind can grant a higher priority to the current playlist than others until the first song is over . A space admin can adjust the expiration time of the temporary priority by using the timeout parameter . Second , state precedence encodes the space - specific restrictions by specifying priorities among states . In the example of a guesthouse , the owner may grant a high priority to the OFF state . It leads Hivemind to turn off the lobby lights unless all visitors prefer to turn them on . 6 . 2 . 3 Putting things together . Hivemind compares scores of three primitives lexicographically . A space admin sets up the priority of the primitives . An example configuration for playlists would be [ state precedence ( on the current playlist ) , fairness , efficiency ] . In this configuration , Hivemind first applies state precedence to decide the next playlist . It then uses fairness and efficiency in the case of tie states . We note that lexicographical ordering could easily be replaced with the ordering of weighted sum . 7 INFLUENCE - AWARE AUTHORIZATION In Â§ 5 and Â§ 6 , we explored our design question on how to make the way of control from being exclusive into collective . In this section , we discuss another core question : â€œ To whom should we entitle / grant control , e . g . , give the right to vote ? â€ . It is about altering todayâ€™s autho - rization model of the IoT actuator , currently based on the physical access to a dedicated controller and / or online authentication of the user who demonstrated the ownership . As a fundamental policy for determining the eligibility to vote , we propose influence - aware authorization . The rationale is to â€œgive rights only to those who are directly affected by the actuatorâ€ . For example , giving rights to those who can hear the music from the speaker , or whose surrounding air temperature is affected by the heater . We observe that many participants spend time finding their preferred values through several trials . Applying Preference elicitation would be a good direction ; it has been broadly studied in Politics , Economics , HCI , and AI [ 9 , 21 , 72 ] . Feedbacks attained upon the changes in the actuator setting [ 34 , 104 ] could be useful in guiding the iterative process . Also , requesting feedbacks in relative forms ( e . g . , â€œtoo hot - hot - OK - cold - too coldâ€ ) would be helpful [ 24 ] . Prioritization of visitors . Oftentimes , properly prioritizing the visitors and their preferences would be more desirable than adher - ing to flat fairness . It would be useful to protect the vulnerable such as the elderly and the sick ; for example , the AC of a bus could be set for a higher temperature upon having a child with cold in the bus , even if it conflicts with the majorityâ€™s preference . Similarly , Hivemind can grant higher privileges to frequent visitors . With their profiles available , how to prioritize them may be open for the site administratorâ€™s adjustment . Hivemind would also provide a reference policy that is acceptable to the communityâ€™s norm . We leave further investigations as future work . Privacy and security . Hivemind uses an anonymous voting sys - tem . Visitors can collectively control actuators without exposing their personal preferences to others . However , this anonymity can be broken when there are extremely few people , such as two . A visitor could infer the otherâ€™s preference from the consensus result or voting status [ 106 ] . We admit that this could be a limitation . However , we note that this privacy issue is not a new problem in collective decision - making in public spaces ; e . g . , we could easily recognize the preferred channel of who holds the remote control . Addressing such a problem would require further research . A malicious user could be tempted to obtain the voting privilege through a replay attack or wormhole attack [ 50 ] . Hivemind would adopt security mechanisms against wormhole attacks [ 11 , 63 , 90 ] . Another dimension of security threats includes antisocial behavior such as trolling [ 46 ] . For example , a group of people could attack the system by quickly and frequently changing actuator states thereby disrupting other visitors . To address the trolling issue , we may adopt techniques in fields of online community and social media such as banning [ 19 , 111 ] , reputation system [ 23 , 75 ] , guidelines [ 91 ] , and moderators [ 102 ] . 11 RELATED WORK System support for IoT ecosystem There has been an extensive amount of work on interoperability of IoT devices and services - messaging protocols [ 92 , 112 , 120 ] , Internet connectivity [ 60 , 125 ] , etc [ 4 , 45 , 55 , 67 , 86 , 94 , 99 ] . Hivemind is built on top of these technologies , and can further adapt for extending coverage . Automaticcontrolofactuators withintelligence . Severalsensor - driven systems have been proposed to control actuators : HVAC [ 2 , 6 , 44 , 122 ] , lighting [ 64 , 114 ] , humidifier [ 1 ] , and robots [ 61 ] . A few systems [ 44 , 66 , 114 ] take user preferences into account . Unlike ours , they only support a specific actuator . Hivemind opens up the channel to explicitly reflect user preferences for generic actuators . Computational - support for co - located public . Technologies for co - located public have been proposed for various objectives : gaming [ 129 ] , sensing [ 18 , 79 , 80 ] , collective visualization [ 124 ] , co - located class [ 47 , 51 , 52 ] , interaction monitoring [ 53 , 54 , 56 , 62 , 69 , 81 ] , safety precaution [ 57 ] , etc [ 20 , 70 ] . Hivemind newly empowers the crowd to control the IoT actuators collectively . Localization and co - location detection . Hivemindâ€™s influence - aware authorization is closely related to indoor localization tech - niques . Extensive prior works have studied room - level localiza - tion [ 12 , 38 , 49 , 74 , 89 , 113 , 121 ] . As we mentioned in Â§ 7 , however , those techniques cannot be adopted to actuators with AoI that are easily affected by the environmental changes , such as speakers . Sev - eral co - location detection and localization approaches have been proposed using sounds [ 5 , 83 , 110 , 119 , 126 , 127 ] . However , they mostly focus on matching acoustic features from ambient sounds , and do not reflect the human perception of a specific sound source . 12 CONCLUSION In this paper , we propose a 5 - layered architecture for social control - and - use of IoT devices . Built on top of the architecture , we present Hivemind , a first - of - a - kind system that enables instant user partici - pation to incorporate the diversity of public spaces , actuators , and users , while seamlessly serving a single representative to conven - tional IoT interfaces . Field deployment on five real - world public spaces demonstrated its acceptance , usability , and usefulness to the public crowd .
