Abstract 24 Small populations ( e . g . , hospitals , schools or workplaces ) are characterised by high contact 25 heterogeneity and stochasticity affecting pathogen transmission dynamics . The increased 26 availability of empirical individual contact data provides unprecedented information to 27 characterize such heterogeneity . However , these detailed data are usually collected over a 28 limited period , and can suffer from observation bias . We propose an algorithm to 29 stochastically reconstruct realistic temporal networks from individual contact data in health 30 care settings ( HCS ) and test this approach using real data previously collected in a long - term 31 care facility ( LTCF ) . 32 Our algorithm generates full networks from recorded close - proximity interactions , using 33 hourly inter - individual contact rates and information on individuals‚Äô wards , the categories of 34 staff involved in contacts , and the frequency of recurring contacts . It also provides data 35 augmentation by reconstructing contacts for days when some individuals are present in the 36 HCS without having contacts recorded in the empirical data . Recoding bias is formalized 37 through an observation model , to allow direct comparison between the augmented and 38 observed networks . 39 The algorithm successfully reconstructed unobserved contacts , and was substantially more 40 accurate to reproduce network characteristics than random graphs . The reconstructed 41 networks reproduced well the assortativity by ward ( first ‚Äì third quartiles observed : 0 . 54 ‚Äì 0 . 64 ; 42 synthetic : 0 . 52 ‚Äì 0 . 64 ) and the hourly staff and patient contact patterns . Importantly , the 43 observed temporal correlation was also well reproduced ( 0 . 39 ‚Äì 0 . 50 vs 0 . 37 ‚Äì 0 . 44 ) , indicating 44 that our algorithm could recreate a realistic temporal structure . The daily degree ( 10 . 8 ‚Äì 14 . 7 45 vs 14 . 7 ‚Äì 18 . 0 ) , density ( 0 . 07 ‚Äì 0 . 08 vs 0 . 08 ‚Äì 0 . 10 ) and global efficiency ( 0 . 39 ‚Äì 0 . 43 ; 0 . 46 ‚Äì 0 . 49 ) 46 were slightly overestimated . 47 To conclude , we propose an approach to generate realistic temporal contact networks and 48 reconstruct unobserved contacts from summary statistics computed using individual - level 49 interaction networks . This could be applied and extended to generate contact networks to 50 other settings using limited empirical data , to subsequently inform individual - based epidemic 51 models . 52 . For diseases transmitted mostly via close - proximity interactions , the rate at 55 which individuals come into contact with each other is strongly correlated with the expected 56 spread of the disease across the population [ 1 ] . In large populations such as cities or countries , 57 contact structures can be approximated by grouping individuals into relatively broad 58 categories ( neighbourhood , age‚Ä¶ ) , and assuming that contact rates are heterogeneous 59 between categories , but homogeneous within [ 2 , 3 ] . In small populations such as healthcare 60 institutions , schools , or workplaces however , disease transmission is affected by high contact 61 heterogeneity and stochasticity [ 4 ] . Capturing these characteristics requires a detailed , 62 individual - level description of contacts . 63 Contact networks are increasingly used to fully capture the interactions between individuals 64 in small populations [ 5 , 6 ] . These networks explicitly represent the links between all individuals 65 in such populations , as opposed to contact matrices which only capture average contact rates 66 between groups of individuals [ 7 , 8 ] . Temporal contact networks further capture the time - 67 changing nature of contacts , therefore representing individual interactions more accurately 68 than static networks [ 9 ‚Äì 13 ] . Contact networks can be coupled with individual - based 69 mathematical models to help design effective interventions against the spread of infectious 70 diseases , since they enable the identification of highly connected individuals who can be 71 targeted to lead to the greatest impact on transmission [ 8 ] . Recently , empirical data collected 72 to build inter - individual temporal networks has become increasingly available . For example , 73 previous studies have used sensors to record close - proximity interactions between individuals 74 [ 14 , 15 ] , and contact tracing programs have relied on the integrated Bluetooth technology in 75 mobile phones [ 16 ] . 76 However , the detailed empirical data required to build temporal contact networks remain 77 subject to several limitations [ 17 , 18 ] . Even studies designed to collect such data are usually 78 limited in time , and may be subject to observation bias ; sensors might not be properly placed 79 to register contacts [ 19 ] , or individuals may disable Bluetooth on their mobile phones at 80 different times [ 16 ] . Due to the resulting missed contacts , the networks derived from these 81 data may only be partially observed . Transmission rates estimated using these partially 82 . Here , we only used contacts 99 recorded between 27 July to 23 August 2009 ( included ) . This period corresponds to the weeks 100 between two sensor battery replacements and hence avoids interference due to loss of 101 contact . A temporal network of proximities was therefore available over 28 days with 102 information on individual ID and ward of affectation . 103 The hospital was structured into five wards : three neurological wards , one nutritional care 104 ward and one geriatric ward . Patients were systematically linked to a ward , whilst some staff 105 were mobile and not linked to a specific ward . To test our algorithm , we used 28 days of CPI 400 data from the i - Bird study , which recorded contacts of staff and patients in a long - term care 401 facility . The observed i - Bird network was heterogeneous , with notably a strong assortativity 402 by ward , varying contact rates between different staff categories and patients , and different 403 contact structures on weekends compared to weekdays . Importantly , we observed temporal 404 correlation between subsequent days in the network , and we estimated that individuals were 405 generally more likely to have contacts with other individuals they previously met rather than 406 new individuals . We therefore informed our reconstruction algorithm with both contact rates 407 by hour , type of day ( weekday or weekend ) and staff category , and probabilities of recurring 408 contacts estimated for patients and staff using the i - Bird data . The resulting reconstructed 409 networks reproduced well the characteristics of the observed network , as well as the specific 410 distribution of unique contacts per hour . 411 412 Similarities between the observed and reconstructed networks 413 The i - Bird contact network was only partially observed , since contact data was missing on 414 average for 37 % of each patient‚Äôs presence days ( 42 % for staff ) .
