DISCOVERY AND FUNCTION OF MULTIVESICULAR BODIES MVBs were discovered following the advent of electron microscopy ( EM ) on the basis of their distinctive morphology in a vari - ety of mammalian cells , including basophils ( Palade 1955 ) and neurons ( Palay & Palade 1955 ) , and also in the alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii ( Sager & Palade 1957 ) . MVBs in the rat ovum were described as “vesicles of special character . . . randomly scattered in small numbers . . . characterized ﬁrstly by the existence within them of numerous smaller vesicles . . . ” ( Sotelo & Porter 1959 , page 335 ) . These MVBs were 100 – 600 nm in total size and contained vesicles up to 50 nm in diameter . The presence of occasional invaginating structures along the MVB perimeter led to the idea that ILVs originate from the endosomal membrane ( Sager & Palade 1957 ) . Over the years MVBs have been visualized in most cells and tissues examined by EM . In the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae —in which the machinery responsible for MVB forma - tion has been extensively studied—studies us - ing high - pressure freezing show one or at most a few spherical MVBs in a cell , each packed with uniformly sized internal vesicles ∼ 25 nm in diameter ( Nickerson et al . 2006 , 2010 ) . In mammalian cells there is more heterogeneity among MVBs . Early endosomes have tubular extensions that mediate recycling of internal - ized membrane and proteins directly or indi - rectly to the plasma membrane , and the vac - uolar region at the center of these endosomes often contains a few ILVs . More mature MVBs are spherical structures ﬁlled to varying de - grees with ILVs typically ∼ 50 nm in diame - ter . MVBs are also referred to as multivesicular endosomes ( Gruenberg & Stenmark 2004 ) and typically correspond to late endosomes as de - ﬁned by the time an internalized tracer takes to reach them . MVBs are distinct from other organelles with internal membranes including multilamellar lysosomes and autophagosomes . Although interest in MVBs has grown rapidly in recent years , there is much still to learn about the role and regulation of these organelles . Variations in MVB morphology and distribution remain understudied both because the compartment is inherently het - erogeneous and because its small size requires EM for deﬁnitive imaging . Proteins unique to MVBs are few because they are organelles through which proteins trafﬁc en route to other destinations and because the machinery involved in MVB biogenesis is cytoplasmic and localized only transiently to the MVB membrane . Among membrane proteins , the mannose 6 - phosphate receptor ( M6PR ) that ferries newly synthesized lysosomal proteins between the Golgi complex and endosomes marks MVBs when present together with the lysosomal membrane proteins LAMP1 and LAMP2 , because M6PR is absent from lysosomes ( Kornfeld & Mellman 1989 ) . Rab proteins tend to be distinctive markers of mem - brane compartments , and several are associated with MVBs . These include RAB5 and RAB7 as well as RAB27 ( Ostrowski et al . 2010 ) and RAB35 ( Uytterhoeven et al . 2011 ) . On lume - nal vesicles , tetraspanins including CD63 are enriched ( Pols & Klumperman 2009 ) , as is the antigeniclipidlysobisphosphatidicacid ( LBPA ) ( Kobayashi et al . 1998 ) . Light microscopy of enlarged endosomes created by expressing con - stitutively active rab5 ( Q79L ) has been useful in distinguishing between the limiting membrane and ILVs and in studying the organization of microdomains along the limiting membrane ( Raiborg et al . 2001 ) . However , because manip - ulation of RAB5 can mix early and late endoso - mal compartments ( Wegner et al . 2010 ) , these studies must be interpreted with caution . With recent developments in superresolution imag - ing , it should soon be possible to provide a more comprehensive view of the distribution , con - tent , and heterogeneity of unperturbed MVBs . Characteristics of Lumenal Vesicles The deﬁning feature of MVBs is the presence of lumenal vesicles . These differ from the lim - iting endosomal membrane in protein and lipid content , and they expand the possibilities for sorting in the endosomal system . Membrane proteins . A large variety of mem - brane proteins are now known to trafﬁc into the MVB in what is the cell’s primary pathway for membrane protein degradation ( Figure 1 ) . The MVB also plays an important role in sort - ing and delivering proteins destined to function in the lysosome . The membrane content of ILVs is distinct from that of the endosome’s limiting membrane , although how this is achieved and how lipids contribute to ILV biogenesis remain open questions . Early studies indicated that ILVs in mammalian cells contain PI ( 3 ) P ( phosphatidyl inositol 3 - phosphate ) ( Gillooly et al . 2000 ) , a signiﬁcant portion of cellular cholesterol ( Mobius et al . 2003 ) , and LBPA ( Kobayashi et al . 1998 ) . Phosphatidyl inositol ( PI ) and the kinases that phosphorylate speciﬁc hydroxyls in the inositol ring , such as Vps34 , are major requirements for various stages of endosomal trafﬁcking ( Fernandez - Borja et al . 1999 ) . Vps34 phosphorylates PI to PI ( 3 ) P in the early endosome , which facilitates ILV formation by recruiting ESCRT complexes ( Hurley 2010 ) . Two other lipids potentially involved in ILV formation include LBPA and ceramide . LBPA is found abundantly in ILVs but not on the limiting membranes of MVBs ( Kobayashi et al . 2002 ) and induces formation of ILVs in liposomes ( Matsuo et al . 2004 ) . Ceramide generated by sphingomyelinase directly promotes formation of ILVs in vitro ( Trajkovic et al . 2008 ) . Lipid rafts may be enriched in ILVs and contribute to ILV formation because of line tension between segregated membrane domains . Cytoplasmic proteins . Finally , every ILV has a lumen ﬁlled with cytoplasmically de - rived material , which provides the cell with a proteasome - independent mechanism for pro - tein degradation . Incorporation of cytoplasmic material can in principle be nonselective sim - ply on the basis of volume or may instead be regulated . ESCRT - independent invagination of bulk cytosolic proteins into vacuoles in yeast and lysosomal wrapping of cytoplasmic components in mammalian cells have been referred to as nonselective microautophagy ( Mijaljica et al . 2011 ) . Selective microau - tophagyofsolublecytosolicproteinscontaining KFERQ motifs was recently shown to depend on ESCRTs , cholesterol , and the chaperone Hsc70 ( Sahu et al . 2011 ) . An interesting exam - ple of how selective incorporation of cytosolic proteins in ILVs controls a cellular pathway is the ﬁnding that speciﬁc sequestration of cy - tosolic glycogen synthase kinase 3 ( GSK3 ) into ILVs ( Taelman et al . 2010 ) blocks phosphory - lation of β - catenin and thereby regulates Wnt signaling . In an independent study , β - catenin itself was found in exosomes ( Chairoungdua et al . 2010 ) , which again demonstrates control of this signaling pathway by ILVs . Regulation of Cell Signaling MVBs regulate cell signaling in a variety of dif - ferent ways ( Figure 1 ) . Signals from activated receptors such as receptor tyrosine kinases and G protein – coupled receptors ( GPCRs ) start at the plasma membrane and continue until ter - minated by either pH - driven ligand dissoci - ation or internalization of the receptor into an ILV ( Katzmann et al . 2002 ) . EGF signal - ing is prolonged by manipulations that inter - fere with incorporation of EGFR into MVBs ( Babst et al . 2000 , Bache et al . 2006 ) . Muta - tions in genes for several ESCRTs in Drosophila lead to nonautonomous cell proliferation ow - ing to reduced lysosomal degradation of Notch and the subsequent upregulation of the cy - tokine Unpaired ( Moberg et al . 2005 ) . The yeast GPCRs for mating pheromones , Ste2 and Ste3 , are degraded in the vacuole in a MVB - dependent mechanism ( Katzmann et al . 2002 ) . In mammalian cells , degradation of many GPCRs is regulated by ubiquitination and in some cases by association with β - arrestin and related molecules that control entry into the MVB ( Hislop & von Zastrow 2011 ) . MVBs also modulate signaling by sequestering speciﬁc cytoplasmic components to tune signaling cas - cades , as discussed in the previous section . Interestingly , MVBs serve as the initiation site for unique signals that are not generated at the plasma membrane ( Figure 1 ) . Biogenesis and Release of Exosomes Although lysosomal degradation is thought of as the typical fate of ILVs ( Futter et al . 1996 ) , an alternative outcome for MVBs is to fuse with the plasma membrane , thereby discharg - ing their ILVs into the extracellular space ( Figure 1 ) . This pathway was ﬁrst appreciated in maturing reticulocytes that dispose of their internal membranes ( Harding et al . 1983 , Pan & Johnstone 1983 ) . The released vesicles are called exosomes . The idea that exosomes might be more than a reticulocyte - speciﬁc oddity emerged after Epstein - Barr virus – transformed B cells were found to secrete exosomes in - volved in antigen presentation ( Raposo et al . 1996 ) . Interest in exosomes has since exploded because of the realizations that most and maybe all cells release them and that exosomes mediate various forms of intercellular signaling ( Th´ery 2011 ) . Notably , exosomes provide a pathway for transferring hydrophobic sig - nals and cytoplasmic material between cells . Proteomic studies of exosomes from a wide range of sources—including everything from cultured hematopoietic cells to body ﬂuids such as urine—support the idea that they are far more than cell debris and contain distinctive classes of protein , lipid , and RNA that also have signiﬁcant potential as biomarkers of disease and therapeutic tools ( Simpson et al . 2009 ) . Many questions about exosomes remain . Most importantly , how do MVBs destined for exocytosisdifferfromthoseenroutetothelyso - some ? At what point in the trafﬁcking pathway do any distinctions develop ? To date , only a few molecules speciﬁcally involved in fusion of MVBs with the plasma membrane have been deﬁned , and no markers are known to distin - guish between MVBs destined for the lysosome versus the plasma membrane . A switch in MVB fate has been described in antigen - presenting dendritic and B cells , in which major histo - compatibility complex ( MHC ) class II is tar - geted for delivery to lysosomes in immature cells but for secretion on exosomes in activated cells ( Buschow et al . 2009 ) . Interestingly , only degradative MHC II sorting requires ubiquiti - nation , which hints at mechanistic differences in forming these differently targeted MVBs . A screen for RAB GTPases involved in exosome secretion revealed a requirement for RAB27A and RAB27B in targeting and docking MVBs at the plasma membrane ( Ostrowski et al . 2010 ) . Whether MVBs that fuse to release their exosomes are fundamentally different from secretory lysosomes that also depend on RAB27 ( Stinchcombe et al . 2004 ) remains to be determined . Exosome - containing MVBs pre - dominate in brain - derived oligodendrocytes , in which the major myelin membrane protein is stored in MVBs until contact with neurons induces exosome secretion ( Trajkovic et al . 2006 ) . The sphingolipid ceramide is important for formation of these exosomes ( Trajkovic et al . 2008 ) , and exocytic fusion to release them requires RAB35 ( Hsu et al . 2010 ) . Inanintriguingprocessthatmaytakeadvan - tage of both the MVB as a signaling platform and its ability to selectively package material into ILVs , an intimate relationship exists be - tween the MVB and microRNAs in exosomes , particularly in tumor - derived cells ( Gibbings et al . 2009 , Lee et al . 2009 , Skog et al . 2008 ) . How speciﬁc small RNAs end up in exosomes is still an open question , but the ﬁnding that com - ponentsofRISC ( RNA - inducedsilencingcom - plex ) accumulate on the MVB suggests a possi - ble starting point ( Gibbings et al . 2009 ) . This is clearly an exciting area for further research . Given that ILVs have a topology opposite that of well - studied transport vesicles that trafﬁc between organelles , fundamentally different mechanisms must be involved in generating them . For many years these mechanisms were a complete mystery . This changed just more than a decade ago with the discov - ery of the ESCRT machinery ( reviewed in Katzmann et al . 2002 ) , and a large body of work now connects this machinery to MVB morphogenesis . Whether there exist entirely ESCRT - independent mechanisms for ILV biogenesis in cells remains an open question ( Babst 2011 , Hurley et al . 2010 , Trajkovic et al . 2008 , van Niel et al . 2011 ) . Discovery of the ESCRT Pathway in Endosomal Trafﬁcking Molecular insight into MVB morphogenesis came in an unexpected twist from genetic studies of vacuolar protein sorting ( vps ) in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae . Characterization of vps mutant strains revealed a subset in which cargo normally en route to the vacuolar lumen accumulated in abnormal stacked cisternal compartments next to the vacuole , which were referred to as the class E compartment ( Raymond et al . 1992 , Rieder et al . 1996 ) . The lack of internal vesicles in this prevacuolar compartment established the connection be - tween class E genes , lumenal vesicle formation , and efﬁcient delivery of membrane - associated cargo to the vacuolar interior ( Odorizzi et al . 1998 , Rieder et al . 1996 ) . Interestingly , exactly why and how yeast [ and other cells ( Doyotte et al . 2005 ) ] respond to the loss of class E genes by forming multilamellar endosomes that do not fuse with the vacuole remains poorly understood and may hold a key to understand - ing the relationship between successful MVB biogenesis and endosome - lysosome fusion . Analyses of class E gene products and their interaction partners led to the discovery of distinct heteropolymeric complexes—termed ESCRTs—that comprise what we will re - fer to as the canonical ESCRT pathway ( Figure 2 ) . Descriptions of ESCRT - I ( Katz - mann et al . 2001 ) , ESCRT - II ( Babst et al . 2002a ) , ESCRT - III ( Babstetal . 2002b ) , andthe VPS27 / HSE1 complex that is now often called ESCRT - 0 ( Katzmann et al . 2003 ) appeared in quick succession ; the complexes are numbered in the order in which they are recruited to the membrane and therefore are proposed to act ( Katzmann et al . 2002 ) . Importantly , ESCRT complexes are present only transiently on the membrane . This is largely attributable to dis - assembly of ESCRT - III by the AAA + ATPase VPS4 ( Babst et al . 1998 ) . Components of the canonical ESCRT pathway are represented by at least one and in many cases several genes in mammalian cells ( Table 1 ) . Characteristics of Individual ESCRT Complexes ESCRT complexes are numbered according to the order in which they act in the canonical ESCRT pathway in yeast . ESCRT - 0 , ESCRT - I , and ESCRT - II are stable multimeric com - plexes , while ESCRT - III is a dynamic assembly of individual ESCRT - III subunits . Structures of all complexes except the functional ESCRT - III assembly have been resolved , providing a solid foundation for current functional models . ESCRT - 0 . In the canonical ESCRT pathway , ubiquitinated cargo and PI ( 3 ) P cooperate to engage ESCRT - 0 on the endosomal mem - brane . ESCRT - 0 is an obligate heterodimer of HRS and STAM . Removal of either protein impairs ILV formation ( Bache et al . 2003 , Katzmann et al . 2003 , Razi & Futter 2006 ) . The complex is an ∼ 8 - nm coiled coil with domains of three short intertwined helices on either end , and it contains ﬁve ubiquitin - binding motifs that participate in cargo recognition , a FYVE domain that binds PI ( 3 ) P , a PSAP motif that recruits TSG101 , a clathrin - binding motif , and a SH3 domain that binds deubiquitinases including AMSH and USP8 ( reviewed in Hurley 2010 ) . HRS also binds to EPS15B in an interaction important for receptor degradation ( Roxrud et al . 2008 ) and to the retromer com - ponents SNX1 and VPS35 ( Chin et al . 2001 ) . Finally , ubiquitin - independent interactions occur between HRS and cargo that inﬂuence sorting ( Yamashita et al . 2008 ) . ESCRT - 0 may form higher - order oligomeric structures on the endosomal membrane that promote cargo concentration and engagement of downstream machinery ( Mayers et al . 2011 ) . ESCRT - 0 plays a key role in controlling the fate of membrane proteins on endosomes . For one thing , it can initiate sorting into the MVB by recruiting ESCRT - I via its interaction with TSG101 ( Bache et al . 2003 , Katzmann et al . 2003 ) . However , ESCRT - 0 also antagonizes sorting into the MVB by directing cargo , such as the β 2 - adrenergic receptor , the epithelial Na + channel , and other proteins , into a recycling pathway ( Hanyaloglu et al . 2005 , Hasdemir et al . 2007 ) . What determines sorting decisions by ESCRT - 0 is not entirely clear , although posttranslational modiﬁcations may provide part of the answer . HRS is monoubiquitinated on its ubiquitin - binding motifs , which results in impaired cargo binding ( Hoeller et al . 2006 ) , whereas STAM binds and activates deubiquitinating enzymes ( McCullough et al . 2006 ) , which suggests a possible regulatory circuit . HRS and STAM were both originally identiﬁed as phosphopro - teins , and phosphorylation may regulate their interactions and degradation ( Stern et al . 2007 , Urbe et al . 2003 ) . Further studies of these and other modiﬁcations are clearly warranted . Infectious Disease Viruses and bacteria exploit the MVB pathway in several ways . The role of ESCRTs in viral budding is discussed above . ESCRTs and other endocytic machinery facilitate the growth of in - tracellular bacterial pathogens such as Mycobac - terium tuberculosis ( Philips et al . 2008 ) . MVBs themselves play an important role in the life cycle of the protozoan parasite Leishmania , and genetic inhibition of sphingolipid synthesis that leads to MVB accumulation is lethal ( Zhang etal . 2003 ) . Similarly , aproacanthocyanidinan - titrypanosomal drug that interferes with MVB degradation is lethal in Trypanosoma ( Kubata et al . 2005 ) . Drugs that perturb MVBs in pro - tozoans may therefore be useful therapeutics . Other Diseases Because MVBs serve as important portals for degradation of membrane proteins , both fail - ure to sort and missorting into them can be pathogenic . For example , mutations in the aquaporin ( AQP2 ) water channel cause autoso - mal dominant nephrogenic diabetes insipidus when AQP2 is missorted into MVBs instead of the apical membrane ( Kamsteeg et al . 2009 ) . Defects in MVB formation in kidney podocytes are a pathological hallmark of focal segmental glomerulosclerosis associated with haploinsuf - ﬁciency for CD2AP ( Kim et al . 2003 ) . Mu - tations in CHMP4B are the cause of famil - ial cataracts [ cataract posterior polar type 3 ( CTPP3 ) ] ( Shiels et al . 2007 ) . The pathogenic mechanism for this remains unclear . Multivesicular bodies ( MVBs ) , which were discovered more than 50 years ago in a variety of different cells , are endosomes ∼ 100 – 600 nm in diameter that contain intralumenal vesicles ( ILVs ) of ∼ 25 – 50 nm . 2 . MVBs play a major role in membrane protein degradation and regulate cell signaling in various ways . 3 . MVBs are central to the formation of exosomes and thereby affect cell - cell communica - tion . 4 . The formation of ILVs in MVBs is controlled by the ESCRT machinery , which also plays important roles in virus replication and cytokinesis . 5 . The ESCRT machinery functions as complexes including ESCRT - 0 , - I , - II , - III , and Vps4 - Vta1 . Each complex performs a speciﬁc task to achieve ILV formation . 6 . The AAA + protein Vps4 is the master regulator of the ESCRT machinery and ILV formation . 7 . MVBs and ESCRTs contribute signiﬁcantly to many human diseases including cancer and neurological and infectious diseases . FUTURE ISSUES 1 . How do ESCRT proteins drive vesicle formation and membrane scission ? What is the structure of functional ESCRT - III ? How does Vps4 affect this , and what role does Vps4 play in membrane scission ? 2 . How do mammalian cells use their expanded complement of ESCRT components ? Do speciﬁc complexes function in generating different types of lumenal vesicles and / or in different processes including viral budding and cytokinesis ? 3 . How does ESCRT - 0 contribute to and potentially control cargo sorting ? Are its putative interactions with factors outside of the ESCRT machinery such as clathrin and retromer important ? 4 . Do cargo molecules control when and where ILVs form ? 5 . How do ESCRTs affect lipids , and how do lipids affect ESCRTs to promote lumenal vesicle formation , viral budding , and cytokinesis ? 6 . How selective is the incorporation of cytosolic proteins into ILVs , and what is the mech - anism responsible ? 7 . How are MVBs that fuse with the lysosome different from MVBs that fuse with the plasma membrane to release exosomes ? If there are fundamentally different—ESCRT - independent—mechanisms for generating lumenal vesicles , do different types of vesicles coexist in individual MVBs ?
