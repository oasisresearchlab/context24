ABSTRACT Background : Superspreading infections play an important role in the SARS - CoV - 2 pandemic . Superspreading is caused primarily by heterogeneity in social contact rates , and therefore represents an opportunity for targeting surveillance and control via consideration of social network topologies , particularly in resource - limited settings . Yet , it remains unclear how to implement such surveillance and control , espeically when network data is unavailable . Methods : We evaluated the efficiency of a testing strategy that targeted potential superspreading individuals based on their degree centrality on a social network compared to a random testing strategy in the context of low testing capacity . We simulated SARS - CoV - 2 dynamics on two contact networks from rural Madagascar and measured the epidemic duration , infection burden , and tests needed to end the epidemics . In addition , we examined the robustness of this approach when individuals ' true degree centralities were unknown and were instead estimated via readily - available socio - demographic variables . Findings : Targeted testing of potential superspreaders reduced the infection burden by 40 - 63 % at low testing capacities , while requiring between 45 - 78 % fewer tests compared to random testing . Further , targeted testing remained more efficient when the true network topology was unknown and prioritization was based on socio - demographic characteristics . Interpretation : Incorporating social network topology into epidemic control strategies is an effective public health strategy for health systems suffering from low testing capacity and can be implemented via socio - demographic proxies when social networks are unknown . Both biological and social mechanisms contribute to this variation , driven by individual differences in host competence , viral transmission efficiency , and contact rates ( 3 , 4 , 5 , 6 ) . For SARS - CoV - 2 , social contact heterogeneity is the primary driver of the distribution pattern in secondary infections ( 7 ) . Variation in social contacts can be represented by a social network , yet public health authorities commonly lack the data needed for social network - based surveillance . However , some socio - demographic variables predict connectivity on social networks and may therefore be useful proxies for the risk of superspreading . Because superspreader infections contribute disproportionately to transmission chains , they also provide an opportunity for targeted public health interventions ( 8 , 9 ) , particularly in contexts where surveillance and testing resources are limited . As early as April 2020 , the Africa Centers for Disease Control and Prevention called attention to the lack of SARS - CoV - 2 diagnostics in the region ( 10 ) . Indeed , serological surveys from multiple countries revealed infection burdens much higher than those predicted based on case - based surveillance , an indication of underdiagnosis of COVID - 19 cases by diagnostic systems ( 11 ‚Äì 13 ) . Testing is an integral part of the COVID - 19 responses of sub - Saharan African countries ( 14 ) , and the lack of adequate testing capacity can impede public health efforts . One way to mediate the limitation of low testing capacity is by using prioritized testing schemes , such as schemes that prioritize testing of only symptomatic individuals or testing of close contacts of known cases ( 15 ) . Testing schemes that take into account social contact heterogeneity on a social network ( e . g . network topology ) could prioritize testing of potential superspreaders , such as highly connected individuals , and may slow community transmission using fewer tests than non - prioritized schemes . As such , they are a potential pathway to epidemic control in the face of limited testing capacities . Here , we use epidemic simulations on empirical and simulated social networks to investigate the effectiveness of targeted testing of highly connected ( e . g . potentially superspreading ) individuals to control an epidemic when testing capacity and social network information is limited . METHODS Social contact networks We modeled SARS - CoV - 2 dynamics using a susceptible - exposed - infected - recovered ( SEIR ) model simulated across undirected networks where each individual was represented explicitly as a node in the network and their contacts by edges . Importantly , we modeled epidemics on an empirical social network , rather than a simulated one , to ensure our results were most relevant to settings with limited testing capacities . Empirical contact networks contain unique network topologies that may be lacking from simulated networks and more accurately represent the true social structure of a population , with important consequences for disease transmission ( 17 ) . However , half of all empirical social contact networks used in epidemic modeling are derived from European contexts ( 18 ) , where SARS - CoV - 2 testing capacities are relatively high . To ensure our results were most relevant to settings with limited testing capacities , we simulated epidemics on two contact networks obtained from rural communities in a low - income country where testing capacities are currently limited . The social contact networks were constructed using survey and GPS tracker data of consenting adults ( over 18 years of age ) living in two villages in the SAVA region of northeastern Madagascar . The first village , Mandena ( 14¬∞28 ' 36‚Ä≥ S 47¬∞48 ' 50‚Ä≥ E ) , as described by Kauffman et al . ( 16 ) , has approximately 2700 people ( based on census data from local authorities ) . The second village , Sarahandrano ( 14¬∞36‚Äô27‚Äô‚Äô S 49¬∞38‚Äô50‚Äô‚Äô ) , is home to approximately 900 people . Data collection occurred over 7 weeks in Mandena and over 3 separate sampling periods , ranging from 5 to 8 weeks each over a 9 - month time period , in Sarahandrano . SEIR Model At each time step , equivalent to one day , an individual could become susceptible , exposed , infected ( symptomatic and asymptomatic ) , or recovered ( Fig . S2 . 1 ) . A susceptible individual‚Äôs probability of becoming exposed is a function of how many infected contacts they are connected to on the network and their transmission rates ( see Supplementary Materials for full specification ) . They then moved through exposed and infected compartments following time - dependent transition rates , and remained recovered until the end of the simulation . Time - dependent transition rates more accurately describe SARS - CoV - 2 epidemic dynamics than memoryless transition rates , which assume transition rates are independent of the time spent in the compartment ( 20 ) . S2 . 2 ) . Evaluating Control Strategies We considered two different testing strategies . One strategy targeted potential superspreaders . In this strategy , individuals were tested in order of descending degree centrality ( e . g . the number of nodes they are connected to ) ; hence , the most well - connected individuals were tested first . The other strategy tested individuals randomly , without consideration of network topology . We only considered passive surveillance , which tests infected , symptomatic individuals . Testing took place at the end of each simulated time - step ( daily ) , after individuals had gone through transitions at that time step . Infected individuals that were positively identified via testing were quarantined by moving them immediately to the recovered class , where they no longer contributed to transmission . While SARS - CoV - 2 re - infection is possible , we did not include this possibility given the short duration of our epidemics relative to immune waning ( 25 ) . In addition to two testing strategies , we considered low and high testing capacities , corresponding to monthly testing capacities of 25 % and 100 % of the total population . To account for imperfect surveillance , symptomatic individuals that were identified to be tested had a 0 . 75 probability of being successfully contacted and tested . Testing began on day 21 of all simulations . All strategies , including a control of no testing , were simulated 1000 times . We evaluated each strategy and testing capacity combination based on how efficiently it controlled the epidemic , defined as the time and resources spent until no infected or exposed individuals remained . We used three metrics to evaluate the outcomes : the duration of the epidemic , the cumulative number of infected individuals per capita , and the number of tests used to control the epidemic . We assessed each strategy and testing capacity based on its ability to reduce the infection burden and the length of the epidemic while minimizing the tests needed . Applying Control Strategies to Networks with Unknown Topologies To examine the applicability of these strategies to real - world scenarios , we compared the effectiveness of the two strategies when applied to a population where the true degree distribution is unknown and identification of potential superspreaders is derived from common socio - demographic variables . First , we fit a statistical model to predict each individual‚Äôs degree percentile in their respective network using the following socio - demographic variables : age , gender , household size , marital status ( single vs . cohabiting / married ) , and schooling level ( none , primary , secondary , higher ) . The model was fit exploring all potential main effects of socio - demographic variables and interactions with gender . A final model was obtained by model averaging all models within 4 AIC units of the top model ( 26 ) . The full details of model fitting are described in the Supplemental Materials . Using this model , we predicted an estimated degree percentile for each individual in the two networks . These predicted degree percentiles were then used to rank individuals by their degree centrality and these rankings were used to prioritize testing in the targeted strategy . All other aspects of the simulations ( e . g . transmission dynamics , control strategies ) remained unchanged . We then compared the efficiency of the two control strategies on known and unknown network topologies , where testing was prioritized based on individuals‚Äô true and statistically - estimated degrees , respectively . RESULTS Madagascar Social Network Topology The social networks from rural Madagascar had characteristics of networks where superspreading is likely to occur . The Mandena network contained 120 nodes and 4136 total edges , with a mean node degree of 34 . 47 and normal degree distribution . However , the distribution of edge weights was strongly left - skewed . The mean edge - weight was 0 . 18 , but less than two percent of edges had a weight above 1 ( equal to 24 hours over a week - long period ) . The majority of edge weights were below 0 . 05 , equivalent to 80 minutes of close contact a week . The Sarahandrano network contained 318 nodes and 16140 total edges , with a mean node degree of 50 . 7 and a normal degree distribution . Like the Mandena network , the distribution of edge weights was strongly left - skewed , but degree centrality was overall higher than on the Mandena network . The mean edge - weight was 0 . 31 , with 614 edges , or 3 . 8 % , having a weight above 1 . Fifty - percent of edge - weights were below 0 . 08 . DISCUSSION In the face of global diagnostic and vaccine inequity , many countries are tasked with developing novel public health interventions that optimize limited diagnostic capacities to control the SARS - CoV - 2 epidemic . Given the role of superspreading infections in community transmission of SARS - Cov - 2 , we explored whether control strategies that consider social network topologies , specifically individuals‚Äô degree centrality , could limit disease spread while using fewer resources than current , non - prioritized testing strategies . We found that strategies that target well - connected , infected individuals are the most effective , reducing epidemic duration and overall infection burden , particularly at low testing capacities . In fact , in simulations on empirical social contact networks from rural Madagascar , targeted testing reduced the infection burden and shortened the epidemic even at a testing capacity of only one test per day , equivalent to a monthly testing capacity of 25 % of the population . These strategies were robust even when identification of superspreaders was imperfect due to unknown network topologies and based solely on socio - demographic variables . This study demonstrates the benefit of considering social network topology in data - driven epidemic control strategies . We find that strategies that prioritize testing highly - connected individuals offer the most benefit in contexts with low testing capacities . Early , aggressive testing has been used to successfully control the epidemic in several countries ( South Korea : ( 27 ) , New Zealand : ( 28 ) ) , and a similar mechanism explains why strategies that target highly connected individuals are so efficient in our simulations . Although delayed testing did result in higher infection burdens for all control strategies , targeted testing remained the most efficient strategy because of its ability to halt the epidemic quickly , even when begun later in the epidemic . At the national or continental scale , it is hypothesized that relatively low connectivity between rural communities may slow the epidemic pace of SARS - CoV - 2 in sub - Saharan Africa compared to the US or Europe ( 29 ) . However , at the community - scale , such as those portrayed in our empirical networks , connectivity between individuals was high . Therefore , at this scale , epidemics may spread rapidly following an initial introduction , and implementing measures quickly are key to limiting infections . Notably , at the beginning of the pandemic in 2020 , many African countries implemented control measures more quickly than European countries ( 30 ) , effectively limiting spread during the first wave of the epidemic . Our simulations show that implementing targeted testing strategies at the beginning of local epidemics similarly reduces disease burdens . While the concept of developing a disease control strategy configured by a social network is not novel ( 31 ‚Äì 33 ) , this study is one of the few that explicitly considers limited testing capacities on par with those in low - income countries . Madagascar tested 26 , 425 individuals ( less than 0 . 01 % of the total population ) between March - September 2020 ( 34 ) , only a fraction of the testing capacity required by mass - testing campaigns that have been implemented elsewhere ( e . g . Slovakia , ( 35 ) ) . This is further complicated by the relative remoteness of some communities , with more than 50 % of the population living further than two hours from a hospital ( 36 ) . In both Mandena and Sarahandrano , for example , no SARS - CoV - 2 testing has been available to date . While cost and physical access to testing are significant barriers to disease control in Madagascar ( 37 ) , our findings suggest that , if and when testing is available to rural communities , targeted testing can mitigate the negative impact of limited testing capacity on epidemic control . For example , antigen - based testing is relatively low - cost , does not require refrigeration , provides instant results , and could be implemented at a local scale via outreach teams of skilled health workers ( 14 ) . Another novel aspect of our study is the consideration of demographic predictors of network centrality to guide targeted testing . In practice , social network data is rarely available and implementing testing based on degree is unfeasible when network topologies are unknown . However , we show that estimated measures of degree centrality based on common socio - demographic variables perform as well as ‚Äútrue‚Äù degree centrality when used to prioritize testing schemes . Health authorities can implement superspreader - targeted control strategies by taking into account easy to measure individual characteristics ( e . g . age , gender , household size , marital status ) , many of which are available in healthcare and governmental records , or can be quickly generated through surveys . The exact socio - demographic variables to include will vary depending on local demographics and cultural practices , and will require input from local experts . However , we found that even imperfect models can inform prioritization strategies if they are able to differentiate the most connected from the least connected individuals , demonstrating that integrating social network topologies into control strategies is therefore both effective and feasible . Our social networks represented realistic , but necessarily simplified versions of true social networks . For example , we assumed social contacts were static and did not change , either as a result of dynamic social behavior ( 39 ) , or changes in behavior due to the epidemic ( e . g . social distancing ) . However , our simulations were relatively short , lasting less than three months on average , and static networks have been successfully used to model rapid epidemics , such as SARS - CoV - 1 ( 40 ) . Our social networks necessarily only included individuals over 18 years old , who represent less than 50 % of the population nationally . Although contact patterns may differ for individuals under 18 years old , our sensitivity analyses found that the targeted strategy was most effective for a variety of network topologies , suggesting these results will hold when applied to the full network of all ages . In addition , the small size of our networks ( 120 and 318 ) meant that epidemics were short and highly influenced by stochasticity , which we attempted to control for by simulating 1000 epidemics with different initial conditions . By using empirical social contact networks , we included realistic social network topologies that more accurately represent exposure risk in rural Madagascar than simulated networks or networks based on studies from the Global North , where the majority of social networks originate from . We included two social networks from rural Madagascar in our study and found that superspreader - targeted testing reduced infection burdens on both networks relative to random testing , suggesting our findings may be generalizable to other rural communities elsewhere . In addition , our sensitivity analyses demonstrated that our results are robust to network size and assortativity , and therefore may apply to a variety of social network topologies . Expanding social network data collection outside of the Global North would allow for more realistic and context - specific estimates of disease dynamics on social networks globally . Incorporating social network topology into SARS - CoV - 2 interventions greatly increases the efficiency of control strategies under limiting testing capacities . Prioritized testing of highly - connected individuals shortens the length of the epidemic while reducing the infection burden . These control strategies are effective even when the true network topology is unknown and potential superspreaders are identified using only common socio - demographic variables . Incorporating a similar strategy could aid countries with limited diagnostic resources to manage the SARS - CoV - 2 epidemic within their communities . Importantly , this study focused on testing to control the epidemic at the population level , and assumes that diagnostics are not tied to treatment . SARS - CoV - 2 control strategies should be adapted according to a country‚Äôs public health policy to ensure that vulnerable populations do not lose access to diagnostics and treatment . While our study focused on SARS - CoV - 2 , testing strategies that consider network topology may be useful for other diseases with similar transmission pathways , particularly when superspreaders drive transmission . This simulation approach could be easily re - parameterized to characterize different diseases of interest . As social contact network data becomes more widely available , considering social network topology is a promising method for allocating limited resources during public health crises .
