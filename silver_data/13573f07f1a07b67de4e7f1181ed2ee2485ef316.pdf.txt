Robot - Supported Information Search : Which Conversational Interaction Style do Children Prefer ? Suyash Sharma s . sharma - 6 @ student . utwente . nl University of Twente Enschede , The Netherlands Thomas Beelen t . h . j . beelen @ utwente . nl University of Twente Enschede , The Netherlands Khiet P . Truong k . p . truong @ utwente . nl University of Twente Enschede , The Netherlands ABSTRACT Searching via speech with a robot can be used to better support children in expressing their information needs . We report on an exploratory study where children ( N = 35 ) worked on search tasks with two robots using different interaction styles . One system posed closed , yes / no questions and was more system - driven while the other system used open - ended questions and was more user - driven . We studied children‚Äôs preferences and experiences of these interac - tion styles using questionnaires and semi - structured interviews . We found no overall strong preference between the interaction styles . However , some children reported task - dependent preferences . We further report on children‚Äôs interpretation and reasoning around interaction styles for robots supporting information search . Children are still developing their problem solving capabilities . This points to the need for them to be assisted with their problem solving . This is also supported by research by Gossen et al . Presently , methods to as - sist with query formulation for information retrieval for children include query expansion and query suggestions , but these do not meet children‚Äôs needs and expectations [ 9 ] . Using a conversational agent could prove to be a good inter - face that children could use to retrieve information as proposed by multiple researchers [ 3 , 4 , 12 , 17 ] . Using a physically embod - ied conversational agent , i . e . , a robot , could add benefits to the interaction as research has shown that more positive effects are associated with an agent‚Äôs physical embodiment [ 5 , 13 ] . We en - vision children communicating their needs in natural language , which is found to be their preference [ 11 ] , in interaction with a robot . However , despite using natural language , present - day con - versational agents , or robots , have not been able to provide children with helpful search experiences [ 14 ] . Current agents usually provide ‚Äúquery - response style‚Äù interactions , in which the child‚Äôs information need has to be compressed into a single query and conversational turn which is often too complex for children [ 17 ] . Furthermore , the ‚Äúquery - response‚Äù interaction style does not provide children with suggestions or clarification questions . It is unknown yet what kind of conversational interaction style with a robot can be used that not only supports children in their search needs , but that is also enjoyable and engaging . Inthispaper , wepresentanexploratorystudy intorobot - supported information search and how children experience this . To the best of our knowledge , the current effort is one of the few ( non - wizarded ) explorations into two different multi - turn conversational styles to support simple search tasks that are implemented on a robot for children . We evaluate these two interaction styles with 35 chil - dren aged 9 ‚Äì 12 years old on children‚Äôs perceived likeability and preference . However , it is still understudied how exactly query ( re - ) formulation and clarification questions can be embedded in a multi - turn interaction style that is both effective and enjoyable for children , nor has it been studied how children perceive different interaction styles with a robot in this context . 3 METHODOLOGY A within - subject design study , in which children aged 9 ‚Äì 12 years old conducted a search task with the Furhat robot , was carried out at two different schools . 3 . 1 Participants A total of 35 participants ( 23 boys and 12 girls ) , aged between 9 and 12 years , were recruited . The children were pupils of international schools in Amsterdam and The Hague ( language of instruction was English ) . Prior to participating in the research , all the candidates had to get consent from their parents and / or guardians . The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the University of Twente . 3 . 2 Study design Children were presented with two different search tasks and the Furhat robot 2 with two different interaction styles . The order of the search tasks , as well as the presentation order of the interaction styles were randomised over the participants . The search tasks were to find information about Barack Obama and his personal family history ( referred to as the Obama topic ) , and about what era the velociraptor dinosaur lived in and what it ate ( referred to as the dinosaur topic ) . The Furhat robot would take input by listening to the user , convert the speech to text , run one of the two different interaction styles , generate a response based on the algorithm of that interaction style , and then convert that response back from text to speech for the user to hear . The researcher requested the participant to start with a specific statement to begin the interaction with the robot - if the task was about Obama , this sentence was ‚ÄúI want to search about Obama‚Äù and if the task was about dinosaurs , the sentence was ‚ÄúI want to search about dinosaurs‚Äù . Each child interacted with the Qulac - based and the ELIZA - based robot carrying out the Obama and dinosaur search task . After each robot interaction , the Godspeed likability questionnaire was administered and an interview was carried out . After seeing both robots , additional questions in the questionnaire as well as in the interview were asked . The results from the interviews show that 60 % ( 21 / 35 par - ticipants ) preferred the Qulac version and 40 % ( 14 / 35 participants ) preferred the ELIZA version . 4 . 2 Qualitative findings from the interviews A thematic analysis was performed on the interviews to identify recurring themes and patterns related to children‚Äôs interaction ex - perience with the two conditions . This could give us more insights into what specific aspects in the interaction children liked and dis - liked and why . We identified the following most common themes from the responses : Ease of use Most of the participants ( 97 % ; 34 out of 35 ) mentioned the fact that they would prefer talking to at least one version of the conversational robot , over typing out information using an iPad or a laptop . Among the 97 % of participants , 71 % said they would prefer talking to both versions of the conversational robot over typing . They gave several reasons for it , including it being ‚Äúquicker than typing‚Äù and that it asked them questions back . A few participants ( 11 . 4 % ) also mentioned that they felt as though they had a friend they were talking to , who was helping them out . There were partic - ipants who had been diagnosed with ADHD who mentioned that searching for information online was harder using a computer , and if the robot was made smarter , it would be ‚Äúeasy‚Äù for someone with ADHD to find information by speaking to a robot . Two participants , whose first language was not English , said that they would prefer speaking to the robot over typing as they were not fluent in English yet . While most participants said they preferred using the robot to searching for information through a computer , there were a few exceptions . One of the older participants said they preferred the robot less than using computers . Their reasons for this included them saying they could find images and search through different websites if they just used a computer . Redundant questions ( Qulac and ELIZA ) In the interview , three participants reported that both versions of the robot sometimes kept asking them questions , despite them having stated what they wanted . They sometimes found this bothersome . Participants said that they would have preferred it if the robot had ‚Äúlistened better‚Äù . Didn‚Äôt understand what I wanted to search for ( Qulac ) An issue that six participants reported about the Qulac version was that it was difficult for it to understand what the children were saying . They reported that instead of responding to what they wanted , the Qulac robot would ‚Äúkeep jumping to other questions‚Äù and ‚Äúgo further away‚Äù . Some participants stated that they felt the system was ‚Äúun - responsive‚Äù to what they were saying . Understood what I wanted to say ( ELIZA ) Two participants stated a preference for the ELIZA version of the robot as it asked sim - pler questions and its ‚Äúreplies were clearer‚Äù . Some participants said during the interview that they were able to ‚Äúmake more of a con - nection‚Äù with the ELIZA version of the robot . Random / Vague questions ( ELIZA ) While there were participants that enjoyed talking more to the ELIZA for its simplicity and clarity , five other participants found it asking a lot of vague and confusing questions . Participants reported that the ELIZA system sometimes kept repeating itself . They also talked about how the system can ‚Äúdistract‚Äù and go off - topic often . And they would prefer using the ELIZA version for specific informa - tion ( they mentioned ‚Äúin - depth searching‚Äù ) as it would ask more open - ended questions that would prompt the participant further . 5 DISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSION 5 . 1 Discussion Using a conversational robot , we explored two interaction styles supporting children in their search tasks . When asked what robot they preferred , children slightly leaned more towards choosing the Qulac over the ELIZA version , while the Godspeed Likability scales did not show significant differences between the two versions . From the interviews , we learned that there were a few reasons that the participants preferred the Qulac version over the ELIZA version , which included prompting good query suggestions relevant to the topic in the form of clarification questions , staying on topic of the search task , not repeating itself . Another explanation may be that close - ended questions may require less effort to answer than open - ended questions [ 6 ] . Participants who indicated a preference for the ELIZA version more often reported feeling more ‚Äúheard‚Äù and found its questions easier and simpler . One participant even associated the Qulac version with ‚Äúbroader‚Äù search tasks and the ELIZA version with more specific search task . So it seems that both versions proved to be useful in different ways , but it is unclear yet how these insights can be leveraged in a multi - turn conversational search interaction . 5 . 2 Limitations One of the limitations in our study was that the interactions were relatively short and basic . While the Qulac version had a set number of clarification questions that it asked each time , regardless of what the user said , the ELIZA version was more user - responsive which might have yielded more unexpected , erroneous responses due to the rather simple rule - matching strategy implemented by the first author . The simplicity of both versions and their errors might have influenced the results . Another point of attention is the difference in search task pro - vided to the children . The Obama search task was found to be more complex by the children , and additionally , some children did not know who Barack Obama was . Although the participants were given enough information about Obama before starting the search task , many participants stated that they would have preferred an - other topic to search information on . 5 . 3 Future research The current research gave us a firm basis to build our future re - search on . Children enjoyed the interactions with the robot , and were able to identify things they liked and disliked in the interac - tions : both conditions have their pros and cons . We will continue investigating the two different conversational interaction styles , particularly , we intend to make the system responses more robust , more adaptive to the user and social cues , and knowledge - based to support the child in a more intelligent manner in their search tasks . Moreover , inspired by one of the suggestions of the participants , we also plan to investigate how the type of search task or speci - ficity of the information need influences the type of conversational interaction style that one prefers . Another point of interest is that while children indicated a preference of talking to the robot over typing , future research should investigate in a more systematic manner why this is the case , by studying the embodiment ( virtual versus physical ) and the input modality ( typing versus talking ) . Finally , we will look more into the role of the robot . We found that the children generally liked the robot , but how exactly does the social presence of the robot affect the children‚Äôs experiences in this context ? For example , how does trust in the robot affect trust in the search results the robot provides ? 5 . 4 Conclusion In this exploratory study into robot - supported information search for children , we compared a more system - driven interaction style that asks closed - ended questions to a more user - responsive ELIZA - style conversational interaction , and found that participants had no strong preference for one interaction style over the other . Almost all children enjoyed talking to the robot instead of typing a query . Children were able to identify positive and negative aspects of both conversational styles . The Qulac version seemed to be liked because of its more on - topic suggestions , while the ELIZA version seemed to be more liked for evoking the feeling of ‚Äúbeing heard‚Äù . Possibly , the specificity of the search task also influences a preferred interac - tion style . These rich insights will be taken into account in our next steps in the design of a conversational robot that can support chil - dren in their information search using multi - turn conversational interaction style .
