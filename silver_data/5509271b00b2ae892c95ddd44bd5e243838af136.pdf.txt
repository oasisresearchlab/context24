It has been repeatedly suggested that ID is subject to a paradox : it is advocated and sup - ported in policy making and high level discussions on the societal role of science , but it is sacrified in the daily practice of research funding and assessment ( Weingart , 2000 ; Woelert & Millar , 2013 ) . Why is ID , at the same time , so requested and appreciated by society , and so difficult to adopt , implement , recognize , and prize by funding agencies , research institu - tions , and researchers ? In this paper we offer two contributions to the large and growing literature that has examined the issue and the associated paradox . We first contribute a technical advance - ment in the measurement of ID and , second , we show a number of empirical findings that increase our understanding of ID , addressing some of the most controversial research questions . On the technical side we experiment the use of advanced text mining techniques , namely optimal topic modeling , to describe and measure ID of research ( henceforth , IDR ) . This follows from a recognition of the literature that emphasizes the limitations of previ - ous efforts to measure ID with the help of metadata of publications , such as classification schemes of journals ( Subject Categories ) and citations . Topic modeling has been applied to ID in a few papers in recent years , with promising results . We extend this line of investiga - tion using a novel and large dataset on one of the most important funding agencies in the world , the European Research Council ( ERC ) . On the substantive side , on the other hand , we aim at addressing a number of research questions that are at the core of the debate on ID . They can be summarized along three dimensions ( epistemic , institutional and individual ) : ( a ) To what extent does IDR depend on epistemic factors , such as scientific disciplines , rate of growth of scientific fields , or age of references ? ( b ) Is the propensity to undertake IDR influenced by organizational factors , such as the institutional affiliation ( universities vs Public Research Organizations , henceforth PROs ) or the age of the affiliation ? ( c ) Is the propensity to undertake IDR dependent , in a recognizable way , on the life cycle of researchers ? Are young , or mid - career , or aged researchers more inclined to submit proposals with higher levels of ID ? These questions are clearly crucial in policy making : if decision makers want to pro - mote IDR , for example to address complex societal challenges , they must know under which conditions IDR is likely to flourish . The European Research Council is an interesting empirical setting for the study of IDR , for various reasons . Its mission is to fund excellent investigator - driven research called ‚Äúfrontier research‚Äù , without consideration of disciplinary or country allocation . Although IDR per se is not the main mission , ERC explicitly encourages application from research - ers that have published in multidisciplinary journals . 2 ERC has gained large prestige for the rigour of peer review process and has established a standard ( Luukkonen , 2014 ) . Appli - cants self - select according to high standards of publication ( Neufeld et al . , 2013 ) ; they come with higher probability from universities characterized by research intensity , large number of highly cited papers and good global ranking positions ( Piro et al . , 2020 ) . A topic modeling approach In this section we examine the large stream of studies that try to define and measure ID and propose a methodology based on text mining . Open issues in the definition and measurement of ID ID has a complex meaning that goes deeply into epistemological and sociological debates ( Boon & Van Baalen , 2019 ; Choi & Park , 2006 ; Huutoniemi et al . , 2010 ; Klein , 2008 ; Lattuca , 2001 , 2003 ) . We do not enter here on these debates but follow the literature in 2 http : / / erc . europa . eu / fundi ng - and - grants / fundi ng - schem es / start ing - grants . 6964 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 assuming that ID is a multidimensional construct . Given the importance of the issue , there have been several efforts to identify , define and measure new indicators that might approxi - mate the multidimensionality of the construct ( Porter et al . , 2006 ; Wagner et al . , 2011 ) , fol - lowing a cognitive approach based on information or knowledge flows between disciplines ( Gl√§nzel & Debackere , 2021 ) . According to a classical conceptualization diversity can be defined in terms of ( i ) variety ( number of disciplines ) ; ( ii ) balance ( distribution of discipli - nary contributions ) ; and ( iii ) disparity ( distance or degree of difference between contribut - ing disciplines ) ( Rao , 1982 ; Stirling , 1997 ) . These definitions may lead to remarkably dif - ferent measures of ID ( Wang et al . , 2015 ) . The pioneering studies on the boundaries of disciplines and the emergence of IDR used a lexical approach . They studied words and their co - occurrence in titles and abstract of papers as the main input , building up semantic maps ( Callon , 1983 ) and examining the passage of words across disciplines ( Small , 1999 ; see the discussion in B√∂rner et al . , 2003 ) . Over time , a consistent literature has defined ID by applying measures of diversity to the distribution of articles and citations by Subject Category ( Carley et al . , 2017 ; Leydesdorff et al . , 2013 ; Morillo et al . , 2001 ) . According to this approach , the journals cited in the ref - erence list of articles are classified by Subject Category and a citing - cited matrix is gener - ated . After defining the metric of diversity it is possible to reduce the dimensionality of the resulting matrix and to project the disciplinary profiles on a map ( Carley & Porter , 2012 ; Carley et al . , 2017 ; Cassi et al . , 2017 ; Rafols et al . , 2010 ) . Following yet another approach , the diversity of disciplines is defined by the co - citation of articles in journals in different Subject Categories ( Leydesdorff & Rafols , 2009 ; Moya - Aneg√≥n et al . , 2004 , 2007 ; Porter et al . , 2019 ) . Once defined the indicators , the next logical step is to define a measure of diversity , so that research spanning diverse disciplines can be identified . The most common definitions of diversity are the Rao - Stirling measures ( Rao , 1982 ; Stirling , 1997 ) , the Zhang et al . true diversity ( Zhang et al . , 2016 ) , in addition to the standard measures of cosine distance ( Por - ter et al . , 2007 , 2008 ) and Jaccard distance . Different measures of diversity place different emphasis to its dimensions and have different statistical properties , which are the object of critical examination . Klavans and Boyack ( 2009 ) have compared systematically the diver - sity measures and the resulting maps and support the use of cosine distance ( see also Boy - ack et al . , 2005 ; Leydesdorff and Rafols , 2012 ) . Ciotti et al . ( 2016 ) criticize the use of Jaccard measure as it is not independent on the size of the discipline . Fontana et al . ( 2018 ) support this view ( i . e . the probability of integration between disciplines depends on the absolute and relative size of disciplines ) and add a dynamic dimension ( i . e . the probability of integration depends on the growth of disciplines ) . These contributions make it clear that the adoption of alternative definitions of ID and of diversity measures may lead to very different outcomes . As Leydesdorff and Rafols ( 2011 ) put it , ‚Äúdifferent indicators may cap - ture different understandings of multifaceted concepts‚Äù ( see also more recently Thijs et al . , 2021 and Gl√§nzel & Debackere , 2021 ) . The Topic Modeling approach to ID Partially in response to the limitations of the literature based on the structure of citations , a recent stream of literature has been exploring the potential of Text mining techniques , applied to the words appearing in titles , abstract , or keywords of articles , less frequently on the full text of articles . By using a variety of algorithms ( in particular , Latent Dir - ichlet Allocation , LDA : Blei et al . , 2003 ) and clustering procedures ( Topic modeling ) it is 6965 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 possible to represent the content of scientific papers ( Gerrish & Blei , 2010 ; Lu & Wolfram , 2012 ; Nichols , 2014 ; Rosen - Zvi et al . , 2004 ; Wang et al . , 2011 ) . In this case there is no need for ex ante classification , as in the Subject Category case , so that all limitations of the classification are removed ( Shu et al . , 2019 ) . ID can be defined directly on the maps of topics . The idea behind topic modeling as a technique for measuring ID has been taken on board by the National Science Foundation ( henceforth , NSF ) in the USA . According to NSF , the advantage of the topic model approach on measuring ID is that its algorithm uses data drawn from the research itself , rather than from institutional structures through which the research was produced ( Nichols , 2014 ) . Lu and Wolfram ( 2012 ) compare citation - based and word - based methods with topic - based techniques . By using LDA it is possible to uncover relationships between topics that would remain otherwise hidden due to terminological classifications . Also peripheral authors with few citations might be found central in terms of new topics . At the same time they call attention to the limits of topic modeling : there is no optimal rule for the deter - mination of the number of topics , the label of the topic is assigned by convenience by the researcher , and the units of observation are single words and not more complex and long semantic units . By combining the advantages of citation - based methods and topic - based techniques , Janssens et al . ( 2009 ) advocate a hybrid approach , using hybrid clustering . A recent policy - oriented report from Digital Science makes a similar point : there is a need for multiple indicators , not simplistic indexes ( Adams et al . , 2016 ) . The literature on IDR has not yet fully taken on board the potential of text - based tech - niques , however . Much work should be done in order to compare the findings from these techniques from those obtained through traditional methods . We contribute to this effort , by using an array of optimal clustering methods together with text mining techniques , in order to obtain a reliable measure . We emphasize that the methodological debate is not just of technical interest , but is crucial also for the hot debate on ID that takes place at epistemological level , as well as in research assessment and policy making . If measures of IDR are flawed , they deliver wrong messages to decision makers . There is therefore a keen interest in contributing to the methodology . Searching for antecedents of ID . An exploratory analysis of ERC data By developing a measurement based on topic modeling we are able to capture those hid - den dimensions of ID that would not be necessarily identified by using journal keywords , citations or journal classifications . This measure will be an indicator of the degree of ID associated with the proposal , interim reports and publications produced by each Principal Investigator . Remember that the ERC deliberately aims to support ‚Äúfrontier research‚Äù , that is , crea - tive and risky research at the boundaries of established research trajectories . It is likely that some pattern of interdisciplinarity would not be captured , for example , by classification of journals or by reference lists of articles . Using this novel approach to the measurement of ID , we are in a position to explore some of its antecedents and estimate the sign and magnitude of the relation . They are : ( a ) epistemic effects ; ( b ) institutional effects ; ( c ) individual , career - related effects . 6966 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 Epistemic effects There are general , high level obstacles to ID , which have been examined intensively in philosophy and sociology of science . Broadly speaking , the epistemic organization of sci - ence in paradigms ( Abbott , 2001 ; Lenoir , 1997 ) and the social organization in communities around specialized languages and laboratory practices ( Dupr√© , 1983 , 1993 ; Whitley , 1984 ; Gallison and Stump , 1996 ) make it difficult for scientists to engage into research that cuts across established boundaries . While there are several definitions of disciplines , there is also some agreement on the pragmatic notion that disciplines represent ‚Äústable systemic communities within which researchers concentrate their experience into a particular world - view‚Äù ( Bruce et al . , 2004 , 458 ) . This creates a tension between the existence of boundaries and the dynamics of science in emerging fields that cut across boundaries ( Liu et al . , 2012 ; Rafols & Meyer , 2010 ) , a situation sometimes described as a dissonance ( Rafols et al . , 2010 ) . Huutoniemi et al . ( 2010 ) make the important distinction between addressing ID from an instrumental and pragmatic perspective ( e . g . mobilizing various disciplines to tackle large societal challenges such as climatic change ) or from a more conceptual , cognitive and epis - temological perspective ( Boon & van Baalen , 2019 ) . We propose to explore the epistemic effects according to three dimensions : ( i ) variabil - ity of ID across disciplines ; ( ii ) rate of growth of scientific field ; ( iii ) distribution of age of references . Variability across disciplines From this perspective it must be admitted that we do not have a full scale theory of the intensity in which ID is practiced in different disciplines and a complete understanding of the underlying reasons . Several authors offered important insights , but no general results . For example Silva et al . ( 2013 ) document high levels of IDR in chemistry , physics and biology , but not in computer science , Morillo et al . ( 2003 ) in medicine and technology , while Qin et al . ( 1997 ) and Bellotti et al . ( 2016 ) report high ID in biology , medicine , chem - istry , physics and engineering . With respect to Social Sciences and Humanities ( SSH ) we know even less . Morillo et al . ( 2003 ) and Bellotti et al . ( 2016 ) report low levels of ID in SSH , but also some internal vari - ability . Garnier et al . , ( 2013 , 2014 ) evaluate a NSF program ( Human and Social Dynam - ics ) explicitly aimed at promoting ID within SSH and STEM ( labeled cross - disciplinarity ) . They find a change in an integration measure before and after the program ( 2004 ‚Äì 2008 ) and argue that without a specific program it would not have taken place spontaneously . They therefore recommend the adoption of targeted funding and specific requirements in grant calls ( see also Spelt et al . , 2009 ; van Rijnsoever & Hessels , 2011 ) . On the other hand , researchers in SSH tend to cite contributions from STEM in an increasing way . Liu et al ( 2017 ) document a sharp increase in STEM citations in SSH papers in China but warn that there is an inverse U - shaped relation between the extent of interdisciplinary citations and the scientific impact . As a matter of fact , within STEM we include a discipline with a strong instrumental and pragmatic orientation , such as Engineering , that , according to Boon and Van Baalen ( 2019 ) , might be mobilized for ID programs of large impact . Furthermore , while in hard sciences such as chemistry , physics or mathematics scientists do follow paradigmatic 6967 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 diversity and idiosyncratic laboratory practices ( Dupr√© , 1983 ; Knorr Cetina , 1999 ) , they nevertheless share several general principles ( Ziman , 1999 ) and are trained to speak a com - mon mathematical language . As a consequence , scientists might find less tension , or less dissonance , in working with other disciplines within STEM than outside ( Fischer et al . , 2011 ) . As suggested by Aboelela et al . ( 2007 ) STEM disciplines share an approach to ID based on scientific hypotheses ( hypothesis - driven ) . On the contrary , one would have a difficult time to identify general principles and prac - tices in SSH . The philosophical reconstruction of common methodologies in SSH , due to authors such as John Dewey or Max Weber , did not become a largely accepted standard . For reasons we cannot discuss here , Social sciences are subject to extreme paradigmatic pluralism , while Humanities are currently a collection of highly specialized disciplines with quite separate epistemologies . According to Aboelela et al . ( 2007 ) SSH researchers share a critical and constructivist approach to IDR . In addition , scholars in SSH tend to work and publish individually ( although with some variability within SSH disciplines ) , making IDR more difficult to achieve . For example , Leahey and Reikowsky ( 2008 ) report that collaboration in sociology is seldom genuinely interdisciplinary . We then explore the hypothesis that scholars in SSH find more difficult to engage into IDR . Summing up , we do not have a full scale theory . From fragmented pieces of related work we however suggest a preliminary hypothesis , as follows . H1 The degree of ID is larger in STEM than in SSH disciplines . Rate of growth Scientific fields are characterized by different rates of growth of scientific production . While in the long run it may be observed an average rate of growth of aggregate production in the order of 2 % , emerging fields may grow at double digit for several years . Accord - ing to Gupta ( 1997 ) and H√∂rlesberger et al . ( 2013 ) researchers in fast growing fields are most likely to use more recent science . The relationship between rate of growth and ID is not straightforward , however . On the one hand , it might be argued , looking at the his - tory of science in the XX century , that fast moving fields have been often characterized by high ID , as in the case of bioinformatics , nanoscience , or environmental sciences more recently ( Bonaccorsi , 2010 ) . Fast growing fields do not have a crystallized disciplinary structure and may nurture several niches of exploration of interdisciplinary opportunities . On the other hand , a counterintuitive argument has been recently proposed by Chu and Evans ( 2021 ) . By examining the aggregate scientific production they argue that the growth in the rate of publication is associated with an increase in ‚Äúcanonization‚Äù , or the tendency of authors to work on issues that are already established , cite already cited papers and to remain within narrow disciplinary boundaries . We test the niche hypothesis as opposed to the canonization hypothesis . H2 The degree of ID increases with the rate of growth of scientific production in the field . Age distribution of citations The literature on ID agrees on the notion that fields of research that cut across established disciplines need a period of time before being recognized and institutionalized . As an 6968 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 example , Brown et al . ( 2015 ) report that it took at least five years before their interdiscipli - nary works in environmental sciences get published by top journals . The studies on measurement of ID have introduced the notion of diffusion to examine the time window for a new combination of knowledge to be accepted by scientific commu - nities and spread in the literature . Much less is known on the reverse phenomenon - how old is the knowledge utilized by those researchers that engage into IDR . Are ideas about IDR originated suddenly , on the basis of very recent discoveries ? or are they originated following a longer incubation period , in which researchers first absorb knowledge from various fields , and then mature a truly interdisciplinary research program ? If this is the case , how long is the incubation period ? Gupta ( 1997 ) showed that in fast growing scientific fields refer - ences are younger . At the same time the literature on novelty in scientific production has raised the propo - sition that highly novel contributions may be characterized by a cumulative large num - ber of citations , but delayed several years after publication ( Azoulay et al . , 2011 ; Heinze et al . , 2020 ; Uzzi et al . , 2013 ; Wang et al . , 2017 ; Zhang et al . , 2021 ) . While the way in which novelty is measured may be in some cases controversial ( Bornmann et al . , 2019 ) , this notion is clearly relevant for our discussion , since it sheds light on the cognitive and epistemic antecedents of IDR . While there is no complete overlapping between novelty and interdisciplinarity , IDR is , at least initially , a form of novel research . From this perspective we may then ask which is the process which leads to the formula - tion of research projects characterized by high ID . We cannot observe directly the thought process of scientists , but we can derive indirect indicators . One such indicator is the age distribution of articles cited by ERC grantees . We are able to extract all references in ERC documents and examine the year in which they were published . Based on the epistemic discussion above , we suggest that there is a need for a long incubation process , during which ideas that come from other disciplines are progressively absorbed and elaborated ( Kwon et al . , 2017 ) . Following several authors ( Lee et al . , 2015 ; Mollas Gallart et al . 2016 ) it is useful to refer to concepts originated in the management literature , such as cognitive distance , which is used to define the degree of diversification of markets and technologies ( Nooteboom et al . , 2007 ) or intellectual distance ( Boudreau et al . , 2016 ) . Institutional effects The literature has raised a number of concerns on the relation between institutional features of science systems and ID . Let us distinguish between funding institutions ( government , funding agencies ) and performing institutions ( universities , PROs ) . With respect to fund - ing , several authors have shown that IDR has consistently lower funding success ( Metzger & Zare , 1999 ; Bromham et al . 2016 ; Kwon et al . , 2017 ) , while Bellotti et al . ( 2016 ) show an opposite pro - IDR pattern in Italy . According to Brown et al . ( 2015 ) ‚Äúinterdisciplinary research is still at the margins‚Äù ( Brown et al . , 2015 , 317 ) . Various explanations can be offered : panel of reviewers have less experience , the matching between projects and asses - sor expertise is more difficult , and it is easier to justify and explain the funding of con - ventional projects . Metzger and Zare ( 1999 ) strongly suggested the adoption of targeted funding programs . An indirect support to this line of studies is offered by Seeber et al . ( 2022 ) , who find that ID research projects are not penalized in ex ante selection within the European COST programme . The reason , they explain , is that COST does not organize the project selection by disciplinary panels , but by variable and flexible composition of the panels , following thematic orientations . With respect to performing institutions , however , much less is known . Universities may be at advantage since they have large variety of disciplines . In principle , social interac - tion among colleagues of different background may be facilitated ( Cummings and Kiesler , 2005 ) . Some studies have compared universities with a broad coverage ( generalist univer - sities ) with dedicated or specialist universities ( mainly in applied fields ) . Bonaccorsi and Secondi ( 2017 ) find that generalist universities outperform specialist ones in research pro - ductivity across all European countries ( Bonaccorsi et al . , 2021a , b ) . In any case , these studies do not measure ID but overall productivity . On the other hand , when IDR requires prolonged interaction over time , PROs may be more flexible in arranging dedicated struc - tures , relaxing researchers from the constraint of teaching . According to Fischer et al . ( 2011 ) the institutional context of the research is one of the most important facilitating fac - tor , but we do not have systematic insights on whether some types of institutions ( e . g . uni - versities vs . PROs ) are more conducive to IDR . From a policy advocacy point of view , we see no significant difference between universities and PROs in supporting IDR in Europe . For example , the European University Association ( EUA ) has called for ‚Äúrecognising inter - disciplinary engagement in academic assessment and reward schemes‚Äù ( EUA , 2017 ) , while Science Europe , the organization of reserch funding agencies and PROs has repeatedly called for more ID in research funding and organization ( Science Europe , 2019 ) . While empirical studies and policy documents on this issue are scarce and not conclu - sive , we find in the literature a strong argument that predicts that the traditional academic organization is detrimental to IDR . In other words , while several authors show that ID papers get eventually largely cited ( Rinia et al . , 2001 , 2002 ; Rafols & Meyer , 2010 ; Kwon et al . , 2019 , 2021 ; Zhang et al . , 2021 ) and researchers oriented towards IDR eventually find an academic position ( Millar , 2013 ) , they also suggest that engaging into IDR at an early stage of career may be risky ( Leahey et al . , 2017 ) . Authors that contribute to the core of a discipline are initially more recognized than authors that cross the boundaries . This is also because researchers engaged in ID ‚Äúhave to meet standards emanating from more than one discipline‚Äù ( Lamont et al . , 2006 ; see also Lamont . , 2009 ) . This means that quality criteria for each of the disciplines involved must be satisfied ( Boix Mansilla et al . , 2006 ) , making the ID enterprise more difficult . Within expert panels members tend to delegate to others the decisions for which they are not per - sonally experts , making the evaluation on IDR biased negatively ( Langfeldt , 2004 , 2006 ) . When evaluators are experts in a field , they tend to select against highly novel research projects ( Boudreau et al . , 2016 ) . As vividly illustrated by Feller , senior officials and uni - versity leaders may publicly praise IDR , but ‚Äúreview panels , program managers , deans , department heads and faculty may ( and do ) continue to march to disciplinary drummers‚Äù ( Feller , 2006 , 8 ) . This is also crystallized in rankings of journals , in which strictly discipli - nary boundaries are embedded , penalizing journals that publish contributions from differ - ent fields ( Jacobs & Frickel , 2009 ; Rafols et al . , 2012 ) . Consequently , according to Carayol and Thi ( 2005 ) , ‚Äúyounger researchers might dis - like interdisciplinary research agendas because they are likely to be more risky and their reputational rewards are likely to be delayed ( even if they may be spread over a longer period of time ) ‚Äù ( Carayol & Thi , 2005 , 8 ) . According to other studies , younger researchers are personally more inclined to IDR , but feel that this may damage their academic career ( Rhoten & Parker , 2004 ; Merkx and van den Besselaar , 2008 ; Hein et al . , 2018 ) . According to Leahey et al . ( 2017 ) IDR is a high risk - high reward enterprise . There might be another factor that militates against the engagement into IDR at an early stage of the career . Bonaventura et al . ( 2017 ) have introduced the distinction between back - ground and social ID . The former refers to the accumulation of knowledge from several disciplines in the mind of individual researchers , the latter derives from the exposure to diverse sources of knowledge from collaborators ( see also Sosa , 2011 ) . They show that only social ID contributes to scientific performance : ‚ÄúLone scientists toiling away at extending their competence into new research areas tend to be less successful than those who instead leverage on social networking to extract new knowledge from collaborators‚Äù ( Bonaventura et al . , 2017 , 7 ) . We might derive from this interesting insight the implica - tion that a necessary condition for ‚Äúextract ( ing ) new knowledge from collaborators‚Äù is to achieve a minimum level of career stability and seniority . In their survey of surgery , Heller and Michelassi ( 2012 ) found that scholars with longer chair tenure experienced less barri - ers to IDR . If this is true , there are clearly weak incentives for embarking into an ambitious ID pro - ject at an early stage of career . A safer career path would be based on gaining first recogni - tion within one‚Äôs own established discipline , and then , if researchers do have genuine new ideas that cut across boundaries , engage into more IDR after becoming established in aca - demia . From topic models to interdisciplinarity The measure of interdisciplinarity relies on the definition of an interdisciplinarity index calculated from the results of the topic modelling applied on the ERC scientific corpus . In particular , after removing the stop - words and stemming the texts , we used the LDA method to extract the topic models . As well as other unsupervised algorithms , LDA required a careful assessment of the reliability of the model resulting from the computation . In this case , we performed a concurrent analysis with the following 4 metrics to choose the best number of topics ( K , henceforth ) : ‚Äì CaoJuan 2009 ( Cao et al . , 2009 ) : minimization of the average cosine distance between every pair of topics . The best topic number K has a minimal final distance between top - ics in Latent Dirichlet Allocation . ‚Äì Arun2010 ( Arun et al . , 2010 ) : minimization of the symmetric KL - Divergence of the salient distribution that is derived from the matrices of factors . These matrices are the re - projections of the documents on the topics and of the topics on the tokens . The diver - gence values are higher for non - optimal K values . ‚Äì Griffiths2004 ( Griffiths & Steyvers , 2004 ) : maximization of the likelihood of the data given the model built considering K topics . This is a problem of model selection using Bayesian statistics . 6973 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 ‚Äì Deveaud2014 ( Deveaud et al . , 2014 ) : maximization of the information divergence between all pairs of topics . First , we find that the age of citations used by ERC researchers working on more ID pro - jects is in the range 10 ‚Äì 12 years . This is not only found in SSH , in which the median age of citations is notoriously larger because scholars in Humanities cite several old or dead authors , but also in several STEM fields . This is in contrast with the evidence found in sev - eral studies on ageing and obsolescence of citations ( Egghe et al . 1992 ; 1995 ; Gl√§nzel et al . 1995 ; 1995 ; 1999 ; 2003 ; Gupta , 1997 ; Radicchi et al . , 2008 ) . While the median age of ref - erences has increased between 1992 and 2014 , Zhang and Gl√§nzel ( 2017a , 2017b ) still find a value in the range 6 ‚Äì 7 years . Other authors find a range between 3 and 5 years ( Born - mann et al . , 2019 ; Roth et al . , 2012 ) and show that papers that are not cited within this short window after publication have low probability to become highly cited ( Ponomarev et al . , 2014 ; Garner et al . 2014 ) . We find that creative researchers working at the frontier and crossing disciplinary boundaries make use of knowledge produced on a longer time horizon . Second , we find that more ID researchers are not in the early days of their career , but in the path towards middle career . Top ID researchers are found in what appears from the data , roughly after 24 years from their PhD , that is , orientatively , in their ‚Äò50 s . This is another striking finding , suggesting that IDR needs a relatively long incubation period , during which brilliant researchers generate ideas for future projects but , most probably , build up their reputation in a disciplinary way . This finding opens a new direction for research , i . e . the investigation of the relation between the life cycle of scientists and their propensity to engage into IDR . This issue is , to our knowledge , largely ignored . As an example , the influential literature on nov - elty in science has discussed the obstacles that novel ideas , many of which may be truly interdisciplinary , find in their recognition from academic communities , as witnessed Fig . 11 Distribution of the academic age of ERC applicants ( number of years since PhD ) by degree of interdisciplinarity 6983 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 by cumulative citations ( Wang et al . , 2017 ) . In this literature , in addition to the defi - nitional problems discussed by several authors ( Bornmann et al . , 2019 ; Fontana et al . , 2018 ) , the issue of the age , or career stage , of authors , is not addressed . We believe this issue is very important and has several implications . Should universities encourage young researchers to engage into IDR since the beginning of their career ? Should aca - demic careers recognize IDR contributions separately from disciplinary ones ? Or rather they should ask junior researchers to gain first a recognition in their discipline and then move , in their maturity , to more IDR ? In order to address these issues we would like to have a theory of how scientists develop their ID ideas , a fascinating issue which is largely unexplored . Are more ID scientists born ID ? or did they learn to become ID dur - ing their career ? On the basis of our data , the notion that interdisciplinary research arises from young research and from young researchers is not supported . Young researchers must first dem - onstrate their contribution to their own discipline , focusing on internal topics , and react to the career and incentive systems . It seems that successful scientists that engage into highly interdisciplinary projects have already established their academic excellence . Whether this excellence has been gained with purely or interdisciplinary projects is not observable with our data and must be kept open . However , if interdisciplinarity would be an attribute of researchers , or a personal attitude , then we would see it at any age in the selected proposal . On the contrary , the degree of interdisciplinarity grows very little in the interval between 10 and 15 year of academic age . An alternative conceptualization might be a life - cycle theory of individual propensity to interdisciplinarity . This conceptualization combines insights on the age of researchers and the age of cited literature . It goes as follows . All researchers are born disciplinary . Some of them may cultivate in their mind very audacious research questions that require out - of - the - box research approaches that cut across disciplinary boundaries . But they do not take the risk of making them the core of their scientific production at the initial stages of the career . They know they are evaluated by senior colleagues on the basis of their contribu - tion to the discipline . If they are successful they gain reputation and recognition . By going deeper and deeper into scientific problems they may come to appreciate contributions from other disciplines . They devote more time to reading journals that are rarely cited by their colleagues but promising . They engage more freely into discussions without disciplinary boundaries . At some point in time they understand that entering into other fields , or col - laborating actively with researchers from other fields , is promising , intellectually reward - ing , creative , and original . They understand that the disciplinary recognition of this work will be delayed , because most of their colleagues will not accept or appreciate the break of boundaries . Nevertheless , they are no longer under the threat of delivering contributions that are immediately recognized by colleagues . They are established . Maybe their reputa - tion may be at stake , not their tenure or salary . An intriguing question is how to verify whether researchers with a strong interdisciplinary orientation had this approach since their early years - but they did not show it in projects and publications . This would require addi - tional research effort . Third , we have found large internal differences between disciplines in their level of ID , with remarkably low levels in SSH . This finding is consistent with the literature that has found lower levels of ID in SSH than in STEM disciplines ( Bellotti et al . , 2016 ; Morillo et al . , 2003 ) . It must be admitted , however , that the empirical findings are scattered and the theory underlying the reported differences is poor . An interesting avenue for future research is to explore the structure of topics in SSH and STEM , respectively , using topic modeling and adopting semantic similarity measures . 6984 Scientometrics ( 2022 ) 127 : 6961 ‚Äì 6991 1 3 Finally , we find weak support for the role of institutional setting in promoting IDR : there is no difference between being affiliated to a university or a PROs and there is neg - ligible advantage in being affiliated to a younger institution . This finding is in sharp con - trast with the claim , supported by the Mode 2 literature , that the academic organization is a major impediment to the adoption of interdisciplinary , problem - solving oriented type of research ( Gibbons et al . , 1994 ; Nowotny et al . , 2001 , 2003 ) . By looking exclusively at research funded by the European Research Council , we do not find any evidence support - ing this claim . Universities , with their disciplinary organization , are home to researchers that engage into frontier research of interdisciplinary type . It is well known that the argu - ments of the Mode 2 studies have been criticized both conceptually ( Weingart , 1997 ) and empirically ( Frodeman , 2016 ; Hessels & van Lente , 2008 ) . We believe our findings add strong arguments to this line of critique . In this paper we have shown how using a topic modeling approach to ID it is possible to identify the content of research projects and , at the same time , build a measure that allows the testing of several hypotheses on epistemic , institutional and individual effects .
