Interdisciplinary research has brought different context and perspectives into the original research area [ 4 ] , complementing capabilities [ 5 ] and addressing real - life problems [ 7 ] by emphasizing and promoting knowledge exchange and cross - disciplinary collaboration [ 8 , 9 ] . Library and Information Science ( LIS ) has been heralded as a highly interdisciplinary field by a number of scholars [ 10 , 11 12 13 ] . The LIS literature relies heavily on contributions from scholars working outside , such as Computer Science , Communication , and Business [ 14 , 15 ] . The interdisciplinarity of LIS has allowed it to serve as a reference for other social science disciplines and more technologically - driven disciplines such as Computer Science [ 16 ] . The integration of collaboration and research across the boundaries of a single discipline produces interdisciplinary areas and expertise that are important for the development of the discipline . Therefore , studies analyzing the knowledge imported and exported between the LIS and other disciplines has gained much attention . Since the path and extent of knowledge flows are largely unobservable in the process of knowledge transfer , it has been generally assumed that citations from one discipline to another can be used as a proxy to characterize and quantify knowledge flows , which provide a theoretical base for empirical studies on knowledge export [ 17 , 18 , 19 ] . Some research studied the scientific trading between different disciplines [ 18 , 20 , 21 , 22 , 23 ] , which revealed the knowledge permeability and presented the pattern of knowledge exchange between different disciplines . In these studies , the application of the â€œtrading metaphorâ€ in citation analysis allows researchers to focus on a clustering group of disciplines . Moreover , knowledge exporters and importers are identified , while the authors only consider the two ends ( exporter and importer ) of knowledge flows . However , there are differences in knowledge diffusion among disciplines in terms of time and speed . Some articles become instantly cited , whereas others are unnoticed for some time before they are discovered or rediscovered [ 24 ] . In addition , due to the degree of correlation , some studies suggested that references from other discipline will take more time than those from their own discipline [ 25 , 26 ] . The time delay has been defined as â€œcitation lagâ€ . According to some specific articles [ 27 ] , the common definition of citation lag is the interval between the time at which an article is published and the time at which it receives its first citation [ 25 , 26 , 27 ] . However , for a discipline , the citation data of all articles published in a given year is less affected by the citation of an individual article . Therefore , this definition is not applicable when studying citation rates of the entire discipline . Actually , in this paper , it is found that the higher the number of citations , the more obvious the citation peaks , which are followed by a decline . The cumulative citation peak can represent the contribution and impact of one discipline on other discipline . Therefore , we define this cumulative citation curve as Knowledge Wave , introducing the theoretical research of citation lag to the category level , and propose the concept of Citation Peak Lag ( CPL ) as a measurement of knowledge diffusion speed among disciplines . In this article , Citation Peak Lag ( CPL ) refers to the time interval between the highest citation year and the observation year . For category A , its CPL can be divided into citing CPL and cited CPL . The citing CPL of A represents the time delay occurring when category A cites the work from category B , and the time delay can be used to measure the speed that category A imports knowledge from category B . Besides , the cited CPL of A represents the time delay occurring when category A is cited by category B , and the time delay indicates the speed that category A exports its knowledge to category B . Our research aims to answer the following questions : a ) What are the major destinations of knowledge trade of LIS and what is the status of these knowledge flows ? b ) What is the actual situation of Citation Peak Lag ( both citing CPL and cited CPL ) of LIS ? c ) Are there any general rules of LISâ€™s CPL and knowledge waves over the time ? Our study makes several contributions : a ) Citation lag is introduced to the category level , and it is able to be applied into more macroscopic scale . The introduction of CPL also leads to a set of methods for bidirectional measurement of the knowledge diffusion speed . b ) This paper draw attention to the knowledge import and export of LIS , mainly focusing on its destinations , proportions , CPL , growth rates and so on . c ) The rules and patterns of LISâ€™s CPL and knowledge waves in different categories and fields are discussed and compared by visualization and several indicators . 2 Related work 2 . 1 Citation Lag Citation Lag , regarded as the time delay between the publication year of a specific scientific literature and its citation year , reflects the time needed for other scholars to response [ 28 ] , and to some extent , it estimates the speed of knowledge diffusion . However , the time delay of citation is not only related to the subject of the citation process [ 29 ] , but also to the nature of the discipline . Understanding this process can provide a basis for further analysis of interdisciplinary knowledge transfer [ 25 ] . A study of the cross - field impact of the physics department paid attention to the citation lag for the first time . Moed et al . [ 27 ] hold the view that where the citing publications originate may influence the successive citation pattern of the field . In his research , citation lag was defined as the time spanning between an articleâ€™s publication and its first citation . Subsequent studies have shown that there is indeed a lag in knowledge exchange represented by citation data between different scientific fields . The study of Rinia et al . [ 25 ] revealed that the speed of incorporating knowledge from other disciplines appears to differ sharply among disciplines . It is generally regarded that intra - discipline communication is more timely than inter - discipline communication , which means the time delay of citation within disciplines is shorter [ 26 ] . The time distribution of references can also be used to measure the speed of knowledge import and export . Amat et al . [ 30 ] measured the speed at which diverse groups incorporate new knowledge through the difference between the half - life and the median age of the references . In addition , Nakamura et al . [ 31 ] introduced the indicator of citation lag creatively . They utilized the citation lag analysis of inter - and intra - clusters extracted by citation network analysis as a new indicator to represent the speed of knowledge diffusion in subfields in the supply chain research . 2 . 2 The knowledge diffusion of LIS The knowledge diffusion perspective based on citation and the maturity of citation network analysis methods provide a good research framework and theoretical support for knowledge diffusion research [ 32 ] . Liao et al . [ 33 ] pointed out that the impact of Library and Information Science ( LIS ) journals can be investigated from a novel perspective â€” the knowledge contribution to peer journals in LIS . The frontier of LIS development and the decline of related research fields can also be predicted and verified through citations . By a nalyzing LISâ€™s research articles published in core LIS journals , Tuomaala et al . [ 34 ] revealed how the research publications are distributed according to the topics , and explored the foci of LIS research and their coherence , changes , and even neglected areas . Through analyzing the author co - citation and bibliographic coupling of the intellectual structure of Information Science from 2006 to 2010 , Zhao et al . [ 35 ] found that web technologies improved continuously , especially at the research front , and at the same time , the network scienceâ€™s influence expands , whereas the AP - L - 4 : Scholarly Knowledge - 2 JCDL â€™20 , August 1 â€“ 5 , 2020 , Virtual Event , China 198 influence of both the information retrieval systems and webometrics specialties seems to shrink . In addition , previous research mainly focused on the disciplinary and national contributions to LIS [ 15 ] . There are many studies on LISâ€™ preferences in citing works from other disciplines to explore its relationship with these disciplines [ 36 , 37 ] . Previous endeavors also further explored the two - way knowledge exchange between LIS and other disciplines , but they mainly focused on the particular disciplines or research fields , rather than the knowledge diffusion speed and subtle changes over time . In our research , efforts have been made to inspect and deconstruct the knowledge diffusion speed between LIS and other disciplines . Considering the broader trend exploration , we introduced the Biglan model , which has been updated and refined to follow the development of contemporary discipline , to map categories to science types [ 38 , 39 ] . Biglan Anthony [ 40 , 41 ] proposed the discipline classification scheme in which academic areas were clustered based on their ( a ) concern with a single paradigm ( dimension I , consisting of hard science and soft science ) , ( b ) concern with their application ( dimension II , consisting of pure science and applied science ) , and ( c ) concern with the life system ( dimension III , consisting of life system and nonlife system ) . Dimension I and dimension II have been widely applied to explore disciplinary differences by researchers from a variety of disciplines , such as LIS , education , and language and linguistics [ 42 , 43 , 44 ] , while dimension III is generally less recognized and less cited than other dimensions [ 39 ] . Thus , our paper also mainly used dimension I and II as criteria to analyze the comprehensive features and dynamic change of different types of disciplines . 3 Data and Method 3 . 1 Data Our research is based on the data from Journal Citation Reports ( JCR ) . According to the research purpose , we mainly selected the data from the field of Information Science and Library Science ( LIS ) . For each year from 2003 to 2018 , the cited category data of LIS which is the record of LIS cited by journals , and the citing category data of LIS which is the record of LIS citing journals both have been taken into account . The original dataset includes the relevant 1 , 030 , 215 LIS â€™s cited data from 40 , 258 journa ls and the rele vant 2 , 087 , 238 LISâ€™s citing data from 199 , 748 journals . Each journal record in a certain year contains the citation frequency distribution of reference object over the past decade . For the main focus of the study was on LIS and other categories , we obtained the basic data of journals from JCR , including journal titles , their abbreviation , impact factors , and especially their categories respectively . This category system coming from Web of Science ( WOS ) has been accepted and utilized in many studies [ 45 , 46 ] . Based on the category - labeled information , we mapped and aggregated most of the journal - level records into different categories . The extracted dataset provides bibliometric data between LIS and other 229 categories from the perspective of time . Furthermore , the framework of the Biglan model was introduced to detect the macro trends . The reasons why we choose the Biglan model are as follows : ( 1 ) The dimensions of the Bigland model enable us to distinguish the 2 key factors th at may affect citation lag , disciplineâ€™s nature and their role . For the former , previous research has demonstrated that there are totally different knowledge basis and research paradigms between natural science and social and humanities science [ 47 ] , which leads to their diverse states in knowledge transfer . For the later , different purposes , methods , systems and outcomes of theoretical and applied research are naturally reflected in their knowledge flows [ 48 ] , and lead to their complementary roles in science world . ( 2 ) The Bigland model can fit our research objectives well and meet the granularity requirements of our analysis . Due to the highly interdisciplinary characteristic of LIS , these categories that interact closely with it are also distinct in their properties , which makes the four classes feasible . The convergence of categories can not only avoid the redundancy caused by the category - level analysis , but also use cluster effects to grasp the overall trends . Thus , all categories were divided into four types of Biglan : Hard and Pure science ( HP ) , Hard and Applied science ( HA ) , Soft and Pure science ( SP ) , as well as Soft and Applied science ( SA ) . We mainly used 54 most related categories ( including LIS itself ) in this paper , which were extracted from the categories that once presented in TOP 30 cited or citing list of the LIS . No matter which direction of knowledge flow is , CPL of soft science is usually longer than that of hard science . In general , LIS absorbs knowledge slightly slower than it outputs knowledge . And LISâ€™s cited CPL of soft science is markedly shorter than its citing CPL , while in HP , the situation is opposite , and in HA , the difference between cited CPL and citing CPL is not obvious . Besides , the change of the average citing CPL is more stable than cited CPL based on our statistics . The similarity of average CV shows that the degree of data dispersion of each sample is similar in our dataset . Thus , we adopted the Kurtosis and Skewness to measure and identify the curve trend of each row . According to Kurtosis and Skewness , the situation of different types is basically in line with that of the representative categories of LIS mentioned in section 4 . 3 . The overall view of Kurtosis suggests that the knowledge waves of hard science are more likely to achieve a higher peak of citation accumulation , especially the knowledge flowing out of LIS , and the citations to HP are more inclined to concentrate on certain years . As for Skewness , it demonstrates the extent of asymmetry of data distribution . Only SPâ€™s average citing skewness is positive ( 0 . 027 ) , which is corresponding to positive skew , but the average citing skewness of SA ( - 0 . 004 ) is close to 0 , which denotes the significant trend of lagging peak , and the skewness of hard science are identified as negative skew . Similar situation also exists in cited skewness . Take citing skewness as an example . As shown in Fig . 3 , it is clear that the citing skewness values of LIS gradually converge and stabilize , and zero acts as a dividing line between soft science and hard science on the whole , which reveals the exactly opposite lagging trend . But the majority of citing citation distributions have long tails on their left at present . Fig . 4 shows the slight growth trend of rest percent ( the ratios of citations from articles published ten years ago to total citations ) . Besides , the ratios of hard science are significantly lower than those of soft science , indicating that LIS cites more articles from ten years ago in soft science than in hard science , which can be proved by the greater distributed deviation of hard science . Based on the view of the references of all years rather than last ten years , we find that there are relatively more citations distributed in the stage of long tails after citation peak in the categories of pure science than that of applied science . However , itâ€™s worth noting that these real data donâ€™t strictly fit the normal distribution , thus we just tentatively use these indicators to probe into their states and changes of knowledge waves . Citation is one of the most effective tools to observe and characterize knowledge trade , and citation lag are also recognized as a common diffusion phenomenon . However , most of previous work mainly focused on articles , lacking consideration of disciplines . We find that unlike citation lag in single paper , Citation Peak Lag is more essential at the category level . By accumulating the citations of papers between two categories in one year , we can calculate both cited and citing Citation Peak Lag ( CPL ) in bidirectional knowledge flows . CPL can not only be used to measure and investigate the speed of knowledge diffusion in certain category , but can also be used to compare and quanti fy a categoryâ€™s relationship with other different fields from a new viewpoint . This makes CPL a very valuable parameter that can help us to set up a comprehensive framework to understand interdisciplinary knowledge exchange . In this paper , we select LIS , which made great efforts to expand its interdisciplinary intellectual impact [ 50 ] , as our research object , and analyze its status in knowledge export and import , including its destinations of knowledge flow and corresponding CPL . Based on our discussion and results , there are four clear conclusions : 1 . LIS is highly interdisciplinary . LIS has some steady knowledge trade destinations , which include soft science such as Business and Management , Psychology , Communication , as well as hard science such as Computer Science and so on . The knowledge surplus of LIS mostly appears in hard and applied science , while the trading deficits of LIS are centralized in soft science . 2 . In the major knowledge flows related to LIS , citing CPL and cited CPL display different scenes . In most of hard science , such as Computer Science , their cited CPL of LIS is less than or roughly equal to their citing CPL of LIS , indicating that the knowledge export velocity of LIS is slower than its import velocity . However , in soft science , including Business and Management , cited CPL of LIS is evidently shorter than its citing CPL . 3 . In general , LISâ€™s citing CPL is a little longer than its cited CPL , which suggests that LIS exports knowledge slightly faster than imports knowledge . And LISâ€™s CPL is higher in categories of soft science and lower in hard science , showing that the knowledge flows between LIS and hard science carry newer information than that between LIS and soft science in the same period . Beside s , on the average , HPâ€™s CPL is shorter than HAâ€™s , but there are some subtle differences between SP and SA . 4 . The distinction of knowledge waves from different typesâ€™ categories not only reflect in CPL , but also reflect in the data distribution . Based on the results of kurtosis and skewness , we speculate that hard science not only tends to achieve higher citation peak , but also can achieve that more rapidly . Also , the citation of hard science tends to focus on certain years . In contrast , the soft or pure science tends to have more severe negative skew distribution and longer left long tails . Our research introduces a unique and effective indicator , citation peak lag ( CPL ) , which can reflect the velocity of knowledge diffusion . Based on the dataset of LIS from JCR , we find out that the more citation data accumulated , the more accurate the CPL is . However , the findings of our work are limited to one research domain , LIS . Thus , we intend to introduce broader data sources and aggregate citation data at a higher level , and utilize CPL to analyze the trends of knowledge exchange between different disciplines . In addition , we find that there might be some correlations between CPL and other metrics , such as Half - Life , which are worthy of further validation and research .
