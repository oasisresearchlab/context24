Abstract 29 Introduction : Understanding human mixing patterns relevant to infectious diseases spread 30 through close contact is vital for modelling transmission dynamics and optimisation of 31 disease control strategies . Mixing patterns in low - income countries like Malawi are not well 32 understood . 33 34 Methodology : We conducted a social mixing survey in urban Blantyre , Malawi between 35 April and July 2021 ( between the 2nd and 3rd wave of COVID - 19 infections ) . Participants 36 living in densely - populated neighbourhoods were randomly sampled and , if they consented , 37 reported their physical and non - physical contacts within and outside homes lasting at least 5 38 minutes during the previous day . Age - specific mixing rates were calculated , and a negative 39 binomial mixed effects model was used to estimate determinants of contact behaviour . 40 41 Results : Of 1 , 201 individuals enrolled , 702 ( 58 . 5 % ) were female , the median age was 15 42 years ( interquartile range [ IQR ] 5 - 32 ) and 127 ( 10 . 6 % ) were HIV - positive . On average , 43 participants reported 10 . 3 contacts per day ( range : 1 - 25 ) . Mixing patterns were highly age - 44 assortative , particularly those within the community and with skin - to - skin contact . Adults 45 aged 20 - 49y reported the most contacts ( median : 11 , IQR : 8 - 15 ) of all age groups ; 38 % 46 ( 95 % CI : 16 - 63 ) more than infants ( median : 8 , IQR : 5 - 10 ) , who had the least contacts . 47 Household contact frequency increased by 3 % ( 95 % CI 2 - 5 ) per additional household 48 member . Unemployed participants had 15 % ( 95 % CI : 9 - 21 ) fewer contacts than other adults . 49 Among long range ( > 30 meters away from home ) contacts , secondary school children had 50 the largest median contact distance from home ( 257m , IQR 78 - 761 ) . HIV - positive status in 51 adults > 18 years - old was not associated with increased contact patterns ( 1 % , 95 % CI - 9 - 12 ) . 52 During this period of relatively low COVID - 19 incidence in Malawi , 301 ( 25 . 1 % ) individuals 53 stated that they had limited their contact with others due to COVID - 19 precautions ; however , 54 their reported contacts were not fewer ( 8 % , 95 % CI 1 - 13 ) . 55 56 . Social contact type , 77 frequency , duration and place have shown to vary substantially between different settings 7 , 8 . 78 Local built environment , population characteristics , and social activities such as traditional , 79 political , religious and leisure events are likely to play important roles in explaining contact 80 patterns variations between countries 9 ‚Äì 11 . Intergenerational mixing between younger children 88 and adults was evident in Zimbabwe , reflecting parental or guardian roles played by adults 89 13 , though age - sex mixing patterns are not well knowm 12 , 13 . Commonly , people tend to make 90 high number of contacts within a short distance of their homes , with this being most 91 pronounced for people living close to their usual place of work 9 , 15 . Where commuting long 92 distances to work is common through mass transport , outbreak containment becomes more 93 difficult due to greater ease of wide spatial spread of respiratory infections 16 . 94 95 Physical distancing and lockdown NPIs to limit the number and geographical spread of close 96 contacts have been used extensively during the COVID - 19 pandemic , especially in HICs 97 and MICs where relatively ; mobility is usually high , per capita hospitalisation and mortality 98 during the first wave was high , and lockdowns were strictly observed 17 ‚Äì 20 . In LICs , the 99 impact of NPIs on social contacts is less clear , with added uncertainty on whether individual 100 contacts are affected by COVID - 19 vaccination status due to perceived attitude of being 101 protected from severe COVID - 19 21 . However , this is unlikely due to relatively very low 102 current COVID - 19 vaccination coverage in LICs 22 . 103 104 Unlike in HICs , urban neighbourhoods in LICs are predominantly comprised of high - density 105 informal settlements , relatively larger households ( extended families ) , have low rates of 106 formal employment , and often high HIV prevalence 23 , 24 . Contact patterns may then differ 107 substantially having , for example , much higher rates within than outside households , and a 108 higher ratio of skin - to - skin to verbal contacts 7 , 13 . Despite these anticipated differences , very 109 few mixing studies are from Africa 9 , 10 , 13 , 14 , 17 , 25 , 26 , and with only three from LICs 12 ‚Äì 14 . Also , 110 understanding how contact patterns vary by HIV status is important to inform targeted 111 interventions to mitigate risks both to HIV - poisitive individuals and to their social contacts . 112 .
